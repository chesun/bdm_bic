## 1. Literature Map

### (a) Empirical Evaluations of BDM Belief Elicitation

**Karni (2009)**, *Econometrica* [✅ in library]  
Formalizes the direct elicitation BDM for beliefs (sometimes called Probability Matching or Stochastic BDM). Proves dominant-strategy incentive compatibility under probabilistic sophistication, dominance, and no-stakes, without requiring EU or risk neutrality.

**Holt & Smith (2016)**, *AEJ: Micro* [✅ in library]  
The most important head-to-head comparison for this project. Uses a Bayesian updating task with urn draws to compare QSR, direct BDM, and the LC (two-stage lottery choice) variant. LC produces the highest rate of correct Bayesian responses and lowest error; direct BDM performs worse than LC but better than QSR. Establishes that the *implementation* of BDM matters substantially for data quality.

**Hao & Houser (2012)**, *J. Risk & Uncertainty* [✅ in library]  
Compares Karni's "declarative" (direct) BDM and "clock" (English auction) BDM. The clock mechanism censors naïve respondents who would otherwise misreport, improving average data quality but losing ~39% of observations. Demonstrates that naïve respondents are a first-order practical problem for BDM belief elicitation.

**Burfurd & Wilkening (2018)**, *JESA* [✅ in library]  
Directly compares three common instruction/interface formats for implementing SBDM. Key finding: dropping the pre-experiment comprehension quiz dramatically degrades accuracy and produces boundary reports. The delivery medium (electronic vs. paper) does not matter. One of the few papers treating BDM belief elicitation implementation choices as a research variable in their own right.

**Burdea & Woon (2021/2022)**, CESifo WP [✅ in library]  
Compares BSR, BDM, and flat-fee elicitation in an online (MTurk/Prolific) setting. Finds BDM is the hardest to understand, no average accuracy differences across methods for induced beliefs, and that incentives help mainly for less-educated participants. Reports that the quality of elicited beliefs depends more on comprehension and effort induction than on formal IC properties.

**Trautmann & van de Kuilen (2015)**, *Economic Journal* [✅ in library]  
Races five incentivized mechanisms against unincentivized introspection in a two-player game. Introspection performs comparably on accuracy and additivity, though incentivized methods are better at predicting own behavior. Often cited as evidence that simpler methods can match complex ones.

**Hurley & Shogren (2005)**, *J. Risk & Uncertainty* [✅ in library]  
An early paper showing failure to recover induced probabilities, with systematic bias toward 50%. Relevant as early evidence of the gap between TIC and actual performance.

### (b) Behavioral vs. Theoretical Incentive Compatibility

**Danz, Vesterlund & Wilson (2022)**, *AER* [✅ in library]  
The foundational paper. Proposes two conditions for BIC: (i) providing incentive information should not increase false reporting; (ii) in a direct choice over the mechanism's lottery menu, subjects should choose the theorized maximizer. BSR fails both conditions. Misreports are systematic and center-biased. **Crucially, their study is about BSR, not BDM.**

**Danz, Vesterlund & Wilson (2024)**, *JEP* [📥 NOT in library]  
Generalizes the BIC framework. Reviews direct and indirect tests of behavioral IC across mechanism types using belief elicitation as a running example. Reports that the most popular elicitations are not behaviorally IC and that incentives *discourage* truthful revelation. Important for understanding how the field is absorbing the BIC concept.

**Charness, Gneezy & Rasocha (2021)**, *JEBO* [✅ in library]  
Reviews elicitation methods and concludes that introspection performs surprisingly well relative to complex incentivized methods. Implicitly supports the BIC concern: if complex mechanisms don't outperform simple questions, their theoretical IC advantage may be undermined by behavioral failures.

**Abeler, Nosenzo & Raymond (2019)**, *Econometrica* [✅ in library]  
Meta-analysis of 90 lying experiments showing people have intrinsic preferences for truth-telling. Suggests that incentive structures designed to prevent lying may be solving a problem that is less severe than assumed, while introducing comprehension costs that create new problems.

### (c) Comprehension and Misunderstanding as Mechanism for Failure

**Cason & Plott (2014)**, *JPE* [✅ in library]  
The landmark paper on "game form misconception" in BDM *value* elicitation. Subjects with a known-value object systematically misreport, consistent with confusing BDM with a first-price auction. **This is about WTP elicitation, not belief elicitation**, but the cognitive mechanism is directly analogous.

**Martin & Muñoz-Rodriguez (2022)**, *EER* [✅ in library]  
Provides a Bayesian model of imperfect perception of BDM incentives and shows that reducing cognitive costs of understanding is more robust than increasing incentive magnitude for reducing mistakes. Again, this is value elicitation BDM, but the theoretical framework transfers cleanly.

**Martin & Muñoz-Rodriguez (2020)**, WP [✅ in library]  
Shows that presenting payoff information contingency-by-contingency substantially reduces BDM value elicitation mistakes and narrows demographic gaps in informativeness. Direct evidence that *how* incentives are framed matters as much as *whether* they are present.

**Drichoutis & Nayga (2022)**, *JEBO* [✅ in library]  
Tests whether cognitive load exacerbates game form misconception (following Cason & Plott). Finds no evidence that depleting cognitive resources increases the rate of misconception, which complicates the "it's just cognitive difficulty" explanation.

**Healy (2020)**, report [✅ in library]  
Provides practical advice on explaining BDM and random binary choice mechanisms to subjects. ⚠️ Full text not assessed for whether this contains original data or is purely advisory.

### (d) Incentive Framing and Presentation Effects

**Martin & Muñoz-Rodriguez (2020)** [✅ in library]  
Most directly relevant — see Section (c) above.

**Burfurd & Wilkening (2018)** [✅ in library]  
Shows that the delivery format (paper vs. electronic) doesn't matter, but the *comprehension procedure* (quiz vs. no quiz) matters enormously for SBDM.

**Andersen, Harrison, Lau & Rutström (2006)**, *Experimental Economics* [✅ in library]  
Documents framing effects in multiple price list elicitation. While about risk preference elicitation rather than beliefs, it establishes the general principle that elicitation format affects behavior independent of the mechanism's formal properties.

⚠️ There is likely additional work on the "how you explain incentives matters" question in the broader experimental methodology literature (e.g., papers by Bardsley, Zizzo, or Hertwig on experimental instructions). Specific titles cannot be verified with high confidence — flagged for targeted searching.

### (e) Belief Updating Tasks in Incentivized Experiments

**Holt & Smith (2016)** [✅ in library]  
Uses the urn-ball Bayesian updating paradigm that the proposal also adopts. The most directly comparable task design.

**Henckel, Menzies, Moffatt & Zizzo (2022)**, *Experimental Economics* [✅ in library]  
Uses sequential signals about the state of the world with payoffs linked to reported beliefs, controlling for risk aversion via the Offerman et al. (2009) technique. Models updating as a double-hurdle process where agents first decide whether to update, then how much.

**Grether (1980)**, *QJE* [✅ in library]  
The classic experiment on base-rate neglect in Bayesian updating using urn draws. Establishes the paradigm this project works within.

**Ba, Bohren & Imas (2025)**, *AEA P&P* [✅ in library]  
Shows that representational and computational complexity interact to produce underreaction to information. Relevant because BDM's incentive complexity may interact with the updating task's computational complexity.

**Known confounds in updating + elicitation designs:**

- The elicitation mechanism's misunderstanding may be confounded with updating errors
- Repeated elicitation within a period creates hedging opportunities if subjects integrate across decisions
- The random-problem-selection payment method (Azrieli et al. 2018, ✅ in library) is needed for IC but may reduce incentive salience

### (f) Meta-Analyses and Methodological Reviews (2018+)

**Charness, Gneezy & Rasocha (2021)**, *JEBO* [✅ in library]  
The most recent comprehensive methods review. Concludes that introspection performs surprisingly well; recommends considering non-incentivized approaches.

**Danz, Vesterlund & Wilson (2024)**, *JEP* [📥 NOT in library]  
The most recent methodological review focused specifically on behavioral IC.

⚠️ No standalone quantitative meta-analysis of belief elicitation mechanisms published since 2018 was identified beyond Charness et al. (2021) and Danz et al. (2024). This is itself informative — the field may be thin on quantitative meta-analyses specific to mechanism performance.

## 2. Honest Assessment of What BDM-Specific Evidence Actually Exists

**The literature on BDM *belief* elicitation is genuinely thin.**

### Papers providing direct empirical evidence on BDM *belief* elicitation performance

(Not value elicitation, not BSR, not QSR)

| # | Paper | What It Tests |
|---|-------|--------------|
| 1 | Holt & Smith (2016) | BDM direct elicitation vs. LC vs. QSR in a belief context |
| 2 | Hao & Houser (2012) | Declarative vs. clock BDM belief elicitation |
| 3 | Burfurd & Wilkening (2018) | Implementation formats of SBDM belief elicitation |
| 4 | Burdea & Woon (2021/2022) | BDM vs. BSR vs. flat fee for beliefs online |

**That's essentially four papers.** In contrast:

- The BDM *value* elicitation literature (Cason & Plott 2014, Martin & Muñoz-Rodriguez 2020/2022, Drichoutis & Nayga 2022, and numerous others) is **much richer**.
- The BSR-focused BIC literature (Danz et al. 2022, 2024) is now **well-developed**.

### What Is Genuinely Missing

| Gap | Description | Closest Existing Work |
|-----|-------------|----------------------|
| **Gap 1** | No one has run the Danz et al. (2022) "information on incentives" test for BDM belief elicitation | Burdea & Woon (2022) come close but conflate the mechanism itself with information about it |
| **Gap 2** | No paper has applied the two Danz et al. BIC conditions specifically to BDM belief elicitation | Condition (ii) — direct choice over the mechanism's lottery menu — untested for BDM in belief context |
| **Gap 3** | The role of comprehension as a *mediator* of BDM belief elicitation failure is largely assumed, not tested | Burfurd & Wilkening (2018) show quizzes matter; Burdea & Woon show BDM is hardest to understand — but neither isolates comprehension from effort/ability/numeracy |
| **Gap 4** | No paper has tested whether presentation/framing interventions (à la Martin & Muñoz-Rodriguez 2020) improve BDM *belief* elicitation | All M&MR work is on value elicitation |
| **Gap 5** | The interaction between BDM complexity and updating task complexity is unexplored | When beliefs must be updated from signals, mechanism misunderstanding and updating errors are confounded — no paper has disentangled these |

### Bottom Line

The white space is **large and well-defined**. The literature has established:

- (a) BDM value elicitation has serious comprehension problems
- (b) BSR belief elicitation has BIC problems
- (c) BDM belief elicitation is theoretically attractive but under-studied

**The missing piece:** Does BDM *belief* elicitation exhibit the same BIC failures, and what causes them?

## 3. Design Space: What Experiments Could Be Run

### Design A: The Danz et al. Test Applied to BDM Belief Elicitation

**Core research question:** Does providing quantitative incentive information increase false reporting under BDM belief elicitation, as Danz et al. (2022) showed for BSR?

**Key treatment variation:** Between-subjects, three arms:

1. Full quantitative BDM incentive information
2. Qualitative statement ("the mechanism rewards accuracy")
3. Flat-fee payment with simple accuracy encouragement

All arms use the same BDM direct elicitation interface and the same induced-probability tasks.

**What a positive result would show:** If full incentive information increases false reports (replicating the Danz et al. pattern for BDM), this would demonstrate that BIC failures generalize beyond BSR — that the problem is not BSR-specific but a general feature of complex TIC mechanisms. If the flat-fee condition performs as well as or better than the incentivized conditions, it would have immediate practical implications.

**Main risk:** BDM's incentive structure is so different from BSR that the mechanisms of misreporting may differ qualitatively, making a simple comparison less informative than hoped. Also, the effect size may be small if most subjects simply ignore BDM incentives they don't understand (producing noise rather than systematic bias).

**How it differs from Danz et al.:** Danz et al. tested BSR exclusively. This tests BDM, which has a different (and arguably harder to understand) incentive structure. BDM misunderstanding may produce different patterns — e.g., boundary reporting rather than center-bias, as Burfurd & Wilkening (2018) documented.

---

### Design B: Comprehension Intervention Experiment

**Core research question:** Can a targeted comprehension intervention (e.g., contingency-by-contingency payoff framing, interactive tutorials, or worked examples) eliminate the BIC gap in BDM belief elicitation?

**Key treatment variation:** All subjects receive BDM with full incentive information. Vary the *presentation format*:

1. Standard text instructions
2. Contingency-framed instructions (adapting Martin & Muñoz-Rodriguez 2020 from value to belief context)
3. Interactive tutorial with worked examples and personalized feedback on practice rounds
4. Graphical/visual presentation of the mechanism

Comprehension is measured via quiz, and the primary outcome is accuracy of induced-probability reports.

**What a positive result would show:** If one presentation format substantially improves accuracy, this provides a constructive solution rather than just documenting a problem. It would show that BIC failure in BDM is a design problem, not an inherent limitation.

**Main risk:** The complexity of BDM belief elicitation may be irreducible — no presentation can make the mechanism intuitive enough. If all treatments produce similar accuracy, the conclusion is pessimistic but still informative. Also, improved comprehension may not translate to improved belief accuracy if subjects' errors stem from effort or motivation rather than understanding.

**How it differs from Danz et al.:** Danz et al. showed that *providing* information hurts. This asks whether *improving* the quality of how information is presented can help. It shifts from diagnosis to remedy.

---

### Design C: The Within-Subject BIC Diagnostic

**Core research question:** Do individual subjects who fail to choose the theorized BDM maximizer in a direct lottery-choice task also systematically misreport their beliefs?

**Key treatment variation:** Within-subject design. Every subject does two tasks:

1. A **diagnostic task** where they choose directly between the BDM event lottery and a number lottery at various comparison points (this is Danz et al.'s BIC condition ii, applied to BDM)
2. The **standard BDM belief elicitation** on induced-probability events

The key analysis is whether individual-level BIC failure predicts individual-level misreporting, and what subject-level characteristics (numeracy, cognitive reflection, education) mediate the relationship.

**What a positive result would show:** If BIC diagnostic failures predict misreporting at the individual level, this provides the strongest evidence that comprehension of the mechanism (not task difficulty, effort, or preference issues) is the causal channel. It would also validate the BIC diagnostic as a practical tool for experimenters to identify and potentially exclude unreliable respondents.

**Main risk:** The diagnostic task itself is cognitively demanding and may fatigue subjects, contaminating the belief elicitation. Order effects are a concern. The within-subject design also raises the issue that experiencing the diagnostic may teach subjects about the mechanism, reducing misreporting in the elicitation task (a positive confound, but one that complicates interpretation).

**How it differs from Danz et al.:** Danz et al. applied their BIC condition (ii) only to BSR and used it as a population-level diagnostic. This applies it to BDM and uses it at the individual level to build a causal model of why specific subjects misreport.

---

### Design D: BDM Belief Elicitation Under Varying Task Complexity

**Core research question:** Does the BIC failure of BDM belief elicitation worsen when the belief formation task is more cognitively demanding (e.g., Bayesian updating with multiple signals)?

**Key treatment variation:** Cross the information treatment with task complexity:

|  | Full BDM Info | Qualitative Only | Flat Fee |
|--|--------------|-------------------|----------|
| **Induced probabilities** (told the answer) | Cell 1 | Cell 2 | Cell 3 |
| **Simple prior** (urns, known composition) | Cell 4 | Cell 5 | Cell 6 |
| **Posterior after signals** (Bayesian updating) | Cell 7 | Cell 8 | Cell 9 |

This 3×3 design tests whether the BIC gap is constant or increases with task difficulty.

**What a positive result would show:** If the BIC gap widens as tasks become more complex, it would demonstrate that mechanism misunderstanding and task difficulty are *complementary* — each exacerbates the other. This has important practical implications because belief elicitation is most valuable precisely in complex settings where subjects must process information. It would also contextualize the existing literature, where induced-probability tasks may understate BDM's problems.

**Main risk:** The complexity manipulation is confounded with many things (cognitive effort, attention, probabilistic sophistication). Disentangling "the mechanism got harder to apply" from "the belief formation got harder" requires careful design. Power requirements may be large if the interaction effect is modest.

**How it differs from Danz et al.:** Danz et al. used only induced probabilities. This explicitly varies the cognitive demand of the belief formation task and tests whether the BIC failure is amplified by complexity — a question the existing literature has not addressed for any mechanism.

## 4. Design Recommendations

### Top Recommendation: Design A+D Hybrid

**Combine the core Danz et al. test (information × mechanism) with the task complexity variation.**

**Rationale:**

Design A on its own is the cleanest gap-filling contribution — it is a direct, "someone needs to do this" paper. It asks whether the BIC failure documented for BSR extends to BDM, and given that BDM is harder to explain, the answer is not obvious ex ante (subjects might just ignore instructions they don't understand, producing noisy-but-unbiased reports, rather than systematic misreporting). This is publishable on its own.

But Design A alone is **incremental**: it applies Danz et al.'s framework to a different mechanism. Adding the task complexity dimension (Design D) provides **genuine novelty**. No paper has examined whether elicitation mechanism failure interacts with cognitive demand of the underlying belief formation task. The induced-probability setup used in most existing work (including Danz et al.) is unrealistically simple — subjects *know* the answer and just need to report it. In real experiments, subjects must *form* beliefs under uncertainty, and the mechanism's comprehension costs compete for the same cognitive resources. Showing that the BIC gap widens under updating tasks would be a result with immediate methodological implications.

**Practical implementation options:**

| Design | Cells | Subjects (n=105/cell) | Estimated Cost |
|--------|-------|----------------------|----------------|
| Full 3×3 | 9 | 945 | ~$11,340 |
| Reduced 3×2 (drop prior-only) | 6 | 630 | ~$7,560 |
| Minimal 2×2 (Full info vs. flat fee × induced vs. posterior) | 4 | 420 | ~$5,040 |

### Second Recommendation: Design B (Comprehension Intervention)

A more self-contained, lower-budget alternative.

**Rationale:**

This is constructive rather than diagnostic — rather than showing that BDM fails, it asks whether you can make it work. The Martin & Muñoz-Rodriguez (2020) contingency-framing approach has never been applied to BDM *belief* elicitation, and translating it from value to belief context is nontrivial but feasible. A 3-arm study at n=105 per arm costs ~$3,780. The risk is that nothing works, but even a null result is informative.

### Candid Assessment of Novelty

| Design | Novelty Level | Assessment |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| Design A alone | Necessary but incremental | Solid contribution; direct gap-fill; not a "first" |
| **Design A+D hybrid** | **Genuinely novel** | Complexity interaction is unstudied; result matters either direction |
| Design B | Constructive and practical | Genuine first if the intervention works; publishable even if null |
| Design C | Methodologically sophisticated | Hardest to execute cleanly; most niche audience |
