## 1. Danz, Vesterlund & Wilson (2024) — Evaluating Behavioral Incentive Compatibility

**Citation:** Danz, D., Vesterlund, L., & Wilson, A.J. (2024). Evaluating Behavioral Incentive Compatibility: Insights from Experiments. *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 38(4), 131-154.
**Read date:** Mar 31, 2026
**PDF:** master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/danz_vesterlund_wilson_jep_2024.pdf

### Summary
Review article. Discusses methods used to assess behavioral incentive compatibility of mechanisms. Reviews existing evidence on belief elicitation.
### Key Findings

- Two types of tests:
  1. **Indirect Tests:** evaluating behavior under the mechanism of interest, or across several mechanisms. e.g. studying the share of truthful reports under a mechanism.
      - evaluations witin a mechanism of whether participants reveal an induced type
      - caomprisons between mechanisms to evaluate which comes closer to truthful revelation
      - assessments of what might cause deviations
  2. **Direct Tests:** Directly evaluates the mechanism's incentives
     - whether participants prefer the designed incentive for their type to the other alternatives
     - whether full and easily understood information on the incentives increases truthful revelation
- Classic experimental design techniques:
  - *design by correlation*: an external measure of a potential driver is used to assess its correlation with the behavior of interest
  - *design by manipulation*, where treatment variation is introduced that will attenuate or exacerbate the effect the driver has on the behavior of interest
  - *design by subtraction*, where a treatment removes the potential role for the driver of interest entirely, holding everything else constant

### Method
Direct Tests of Beahvioral Incentive Compatibility
- **Incentives-only test**: presents participants with a pure choice over the incentives available under the mechanism and evaluates whether most participants select the presumed maximizer
- **Info/no-info test**: uses design by subtraction to evaluate whether participants are more likely to reveal their induced type truthfully when provided with clear quantitative information on the incentives
They correspond to the two weak conditions for BIC by Danz, Vesterlund, and Wilson (2022).

### Relevance to Our Project
Benoit, Dubra, and Romagnoli (2022) is mentioned to assess the role of participants' preference for events they control. They use an elicitation over the respondent's confidence that they are above the median for performance on a task, using a probabilitic BDM (which is the mechanism we are interested in). They find evidence that "preference for control is driving false reports, as reports exhibiting self-confidence decrease substantially in the treatment without the control motive".


### CS Comments
In the conclusion, they mention that "Danz, Vesterlund, and Wilson (2024) show for the most used belief elicitation mechanisms (the classic and binarized quadratic scoring rule, and the probabilistic Becker-DeGroot-Marschak rule) that participants largely prefer payoffs different from the intended maximizer under the mechanism, and that information on the incentives increases the rate of false reports."
This suggests they have already done a BIC test on BDM, but that working paper is not available yet, it is listed on David Danz's website as "The Pure-Incentives Test: Applications to Proper Scoring Rules, Auctions, and Matching Markets".

We should implement direct tests for BDM for BIC for the two weak requirements:
- when presented with pure incentives, choose the maximizer (what does this look like for BDM?)
- info on incentives should increase truthful reporting relative to no info.

### Open Questions

- The forthcoming Danz, Vesterlund & Wilson working paper on the "pure-incentives test" for BDM belief elicitation is the most direct scooping risk for our project. What exactly do they test, and how does their approach differ from ours? We need to monitor David Danz's website for the release.
- The JEP survey discusses both indirect and direct BIC tests. Our design uses induced probabilities (an indirect test). Would adding a direct test (incentives-only choice task) to our design strengthen the contribution, or would it merely replicate what Danz et al. are already doing?
- The "design by subtraction" approach (removing a potential driver entirely) maps directly onto our treatment arms. Can we frame each of our hypothesis-specific treatments as a subtraction design in Danz et al.'s taxonomy?

## 2. Brown, Liu & Tsoi (2026) — Simplicity Refinements in Value Elicitation

**Citation:** Brown, A.L., Liu, J., & Tsoi, M. (2026). Simplicity Refinements and Value Elicitation: What Outperforms the BDM on Initial Play? Working Paper, March 2026. SSRN WP #4476764.
**Read date:** April 1, 2026
**PDF:** master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/brown_liu_tsoi_2023_simplicity_refinements.pdf

### Summary
Value elicitation. Tests 3 simplicity refinements in a standardized 2x3 between-subjects design: CBC (contingency-by-contingency, from Martin & Munoz-Rodriguez 2022) applied to BDM; OSP (obviously strategy-proof, from Li 2017) applied to descending clock; GSO (game-structure obviousness, = UJS from Chakraborty & Kendall 2025) applied to descending price list. Each refinement is compared to an unrefined version of the same format.

### Key Findings

- The base price list (not GSO) significantly outperforms the base BDM (Not CBC) and base clock (not OSP). Nearly half of subjects in the PL (NOT GSO) sessions bid optimally in first round play, whereas only 26% in BDM (NOT CBC) and even fewer in CLOCK (NOT OSP).
- PL (GSO) and PL (NOT GSO) have similar rates of optimal bids. In fact, none of the refined mechanisms provide significantly higher rates of optimal bids. It appears the PL method rather than the GSO prooperty is responsible.
- In terms of absolute deviation from the optimal bid, the PL mechanisms do not significantly differ from the BDM in initial play.
  - BDM based mechanisms appear to be bounded largely by first price auction solutions
  - PL or CLock mechanisms have suboptimal bids congregate near the maximum bid
  - By second round, PL based mechanisms greatly ouperform al other mechanisms on both metrics
  - PL is thus also superior at facilitating learning
- The CBC method of simplifying BDM is only effective when possible bids and states are small (<=7)
- OSP is found to improve bidding in multiplayer auctions, but in the single player environment of this paper, does not seem to do so.

### Method
2x3 between subject design.

### Relevance to Our Project
- These results are for VALUE elicitation, but they carry strong implications for BELIEF elicitation BIC testing. The central finding -- that simplicity refinements (CBC, OSP, GSO) do not outperform plain price lists -- suggests that the FORMAT of the mechanism (price list vs. single-response vs. clock) matters more than its formal simplicity properties. For our BDM BIC project, this implies that testing BDM against an MPL format (rather than against a theoretically "simpler" BDM variant) is the right comparison.
- The finding that BDM bids cluster around first-price auction solutions is directly relevant to our Hypothesis A (comprehension / game-form misconception). If subjects confuse BDM-for-values with a first-price auction, they may analogously confuse BDM-for-beliefs with a different game form. Identifying the specific misconception in beliefs (the analogue of FPA confusion) is an open question our project can address.
- The CBC result (effective only with small state spaces, <=7) has design implications: if we use a belief grid with many possible probability reports, CBC-style decomposition of BDM will not help. But if we coarsen to a small number of bins, it might. This connects to Healy & Leo's recommendation to use coarse elicitation to strengthen incentives.
- The PL superiority in facilitating learning suggests that if we include repeated rounds, an MPL baseline will converge faster than BDM, which could confound treatment comparisons in later rounds.

### CS Comments
- On CBC simplification for beliefs: If we elicit beliefs over a fine grid of probabilities (i.e. 1, 2, ..., 99%), then CBC would likely fail, because the paper says CBC refinements only works for small state space with <=7 states. We could still try, and the results would be interesting, but the hypothesis itself would not be interesting, and the implementation is not straightforward.
- There are also multiple ways of implementing PL. For example, a big list, or a dynamic PL with corase intervals first, then a "drill down" list with finer intervals. It is also an issue of what kind of instructions to give subjects. This is something I struggled a lot with in my previous JMP design where I used MPL. The instructions were cumbersome and subjects reported confusion. We need to find existing instructions from published papers, and identify the canonical instructions that work well to use as benchmark. Same for single report BDM.
### Open Questions

- Would the CBC simplification of BDM-for-beliefs (presenting each possible random probability cutoff as a separate binary choice) help more in the belief context than in the value context, given that belief tasks already require probabilistic reasoning?
- The PL's learning advantage raises a design question: should we compare mechanisms in first-round play only (to capture comprehension differences) or across rounds (to capture learning differences)?

## 3. Healy & Leo (2025) — Belief Elicitation: A User's Guide

**Citation:** Healy, P.J. & Leo, G. (2025). Belief Elicitation: A User's Guide. Chapter 3 in *Handbook of Experimental Methodology* (eds. Snowberg & Yariv), Elsevier.
**Read date:** Mar 29, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Chapter-3-healy_leo_belief_elicitation_guide.pdf`

### Summary
Comprehensive handbook chapter surveying all incentive-compatible methods for eliciting beliefs. Covers theoretical foundations (what a belief is, when beliefs exist and are unique), the two main families of mechanisms (BDM/MPL vs. scoring rules), and practical guidance for experimenters. Organized as a "choose your own adventure" reference by statistic to elicit.

### Key Findings

- **IC assumption hierarchy:** MPL/BDM require only *statewise monotonicity* (Axiom 5) -- the weakest assumption, same as needed for any random-round payment. Scoring rules require the strictly stronger *subjective-objective reduction* (Axiom 6). Thus MPL/BDM are theoretically superior to binarized scoring rules in IC terms.
- **BDM as hidden MPL:** The single-response BDM can be understood as an MPL with randomly selected row. Useful pedagogically; the BDM reports a single number p and a random draw r determines whether the subject gets the bet or the lottery.
- **BQSR has strongest uniform incentives:** Among all proper binarized scoring rules, the BQSR maximizes the minimum strength of truth-telling incentives (G''(p) = 2 for all p). But it requires S-O reduction.
- **Incentives vs. no incentives:** Evidence is mixed but mostly favors using incentives. Key nuance: providing on-screen calculators for BQSR incentives *increases* misreporting (Danz et al. 2022), possibly because subjects exploit the asymmetry in payment probabilities to equalize across states, pushing toward 50%.
- **Practical recommendations:** (1) Use incentives. (2) Do NOT put incentive details on the decision screen -- explain in instructions only. (3) Tell subjects truth-telling is optimal. (4) Consider coarse elicitation to strengthen incentives. (5) For beliefs about a number, avoid eliciting the mean; prefer mode or median. (6) For frequency beliefs, consider eliciting the probability of a single random draw instead.
- **BDM/MPL reduce hedging incentives** (unlike BQSR), because performance on the elicitation is independent of the event outcome in certain branches.
- **MPL/BDM have half the incentive strength of BQSR** (reduced BDM has G''(p) = 1 vs. 2 for BQSR).

### Method
Literature review / handbook chapter. No original data.

### Relevance to Our Project
- Central reference for the theoretical IC foundations of BDM belief elicitation. The hierarchy of assumptions (statewise monotonicity < S-O reduction < risk-neutral EU) directly structures our paper's framing of what BDM requires vs. what BQSR requires.
- The observation that BDM is IC under the *weakest* assumption makes our finding that it fails BIC more puzzling and interesting -- the failure must come from behavioral/cognitive channels, not from strong preference assumptions.
- The recommendation to NOT show incentives on-screen is relevant to our Hypothesis A (comprehension). Healy & Leo note that showing incentives can backfire, which complicates the interpretation of information treatments.
- The "BDM as hidden MPL" framing connects to Chakraborty & Kendall's UJS result that the generalized MPL is the UJS mechanism class -- the MPL format may be behaviorally easier precisely because it is UJS.

### CS Comments
Danz et al 2024 actually recommends against "don't show incentives", see conclusion.

### Open Questions

- How does the "don't show incentives on screen" recommendation interact with our info treatment? If showing incentives can increase misreporting (as in Danz et al. 2022 with BQSR), could a similar effect occur with BDM?

## 4. Leo & Stelnicki (2025) — Eliciting Subjective Real-Valued Beliefs

**Citation:** Leo, G. & Stelnicki, S. (2025). Eliciting Subjective Real-Valued Beliefs. *Experimental Economics*, 28, 880-899.
**Read date:** Mar 29, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/leo_stelnicki_2025_eliciting-subjective-real-valued-beliefs.pdf`

### Summary
Introduces the *quantile price list* (QPL) methodology for eliciting quantiles of a subjective belief distribution over real-valued random variables. Each QPL is a standard MPL where the subject chooses between an objective lottery (e.g., $10 with 75% chance) and an act that pays if X <= x. The switch point identifies a quantile of the CDF. Eliciting multiple quantiles (e.g., 0.25, 0.50, 0.75) allows approximating the entire CDF via maximum entropy. Proof-of-concept experiment on beliefs about math task performance (N=158, Ohio State).

### Key Findings

- **IC assumptions:** Requires four axioms -- objective lottery monotonicity, act monotonicity, continuity, and a *replacement* axiom (that willingness to substitute between acts and lotteries does not depend on the arbitrarily chosen outcome used for incentivization). Plus statewise monotonicity for random-round payment. Notably does NOT require S-O reduction.
- **Maximum entropy approximation:** Given elicited quantile intervals, the max-entropy CDF is a piecewise linear interpolation through the quantile points. Moments (mean, variance) can be computed from the approximated CDF.
- **Comparison with probability elicitation:** The approximated CDFs from quantile elicitation are much "flatter" (more dispersed) than the implied binomial distributions from a single probability elicitation (mean MPL). This suggests subjects' beliefs are not well characterized by a single point probability.
- **Quantiles vs. moments:** Quantiles are more fundamental than moments -- moments may not exist for extreme distributions, cannot always be elicited in isolation (e.g., variance requires knowing the mean), and the incentives for moments are harder to communicate.
- **Order effects:** Whether quantiles or the mean probability was elicited first did not significantly affect consistency, though there was a non-significant trend toward greater consistency in the mean-first condition.

### Method
Between-subject experiment (mean-first vs. mean-last). 158 participants, in-person at Ohio State. Four MPLs per subject (three QPLs at 0.25/0.50/0.75 quantiles + one standard probability MPL). Single switching enforced. One of four MPLs randomly chosen for payment.

### Relevance to Our Project
- Demonstrates a belief elicitation method in the BDM/MPL family that is IC under weaker assumptions than BQSR. The QPL is IC under the same statewise monotonicity as standard BDM, plus the replacement axiom.
- The finding that elicited belief distributions are much flatter than implied by a single probability report is relevant to measurement error concerns (Gillen et al. 2019) -- a single BDM probability report may not capture the full picture.
- The method itself is not what we are testing, but the paper's discussion of IC assumptions (especially the replacement axiom and its relationship to the standard BDM assumptions) helps map the theoretical landscape our paper sits in.

### CS Comments
Ignore quantile price list, not within our scope.

### Open Questions

- Could quantile price lists be used as a robustness check in our design -- e.g., eliciting the full belief distribution in addition to the single probability, to see if BIC failures are related to the flatness of the true distribution?

## 5. Grapow (2026) — Eliciting Belief Distributions: A Comparative Study

**Citation:** Grapow, H. (2026). Eliciting Belief Distributions: A Comparative Study. Working Paper, Ghent University (RISLab).
**Read date:** Mar 29, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Grapow_Eliciting Belief Distributions A Comparative Study.pdf`

### Summary
Compares three methods for eliciting individual-level belief distributions in strategic environments (Beauty Contest game, Ultimatum game): (1) Bet-Based Method (BB) -- binary choices between bets on events, IC under Biseparable Preference Model (Ghirardato & Marinacci 2001) and Smooth Model (Klibanoff et al. 2005); (2) Money Method (MM) -- binary choices between a sure amount and a bet on an event, IC under Biseparable model, recovers beliefs AND preferences jointly; (3) Introspective Method -- unincentivized survey ("what is the percent chance..."), requires truthful reporting assumption. Beliefs assumed to follow Beta distribution (BB, MM) or log-normal (Introspective). Estimated via Bayesian hierarchical models.

### Key Findings

- **All three methods recover reliable beliefs** in both strategic settings across five evaluation metrics.
- **Non-parametric results:** BB has good response times and monotonicity; MM has longer response times (3.1s vs. 3.9s per choice) and more tasks (161 vs. 39 binary choices for Exp. 1) but consistent choices; Introspective is fastest (~13s total) but has monotonicity issues.
- **Parametric results:** Both mean belief and belief confidence significantly predict choices in the Beauty Contest. In the Ultimatum game, belief confidence is particularly important for predicting deviations from best responses.
- **MM performance depends on decision model assumed:** Probability Weighting models fit substantially better than Subjective Expected Utility. This matters because MM requires specifying functional forms for both beliefs and preferences.
- **BB is recommended** when goal is to recover beliefs with minimal functional form assumptions. MM is preferred when ambiguity attitudes are also of interest. Introspective is suitable when response time is the binding constraint.
- **Key tradeoff:** BB and MM both use non-chained independent binary choices (avoiding error accumulation and strategic responding inherent in choice lists), but require complex econometrics (hierarchical Bayesian) to recover distributions. Introspective is simple but relies on understanding of probability concepts.

### Method
Two between-subject online experiments on Prolific (US pool). Exp. 1: Beauty Contest (N=190; 98 BB, 92 MM). Exp. 2: Ultimatum game (N=198; ~100 per treatment). Five evaluation metrics: response time, consistency, monotonicity (non-parametric); calibration, predictive accuracy (parametric).

### Relevance to Our Project
- The paper's finding that all methods (including unincentivized introspective) recover reliable beliefs is relevant context for our project: if even unincentivized methods work reasonably well, BIC failures of incentivized mechanisms may matter less than commonly assumed -- OR they suggest the incentivized mechanisms add complexity that offsets their theoretical advantages (supporting Hypothesis A: comprehension).
- The emphasis on non-chained binary choices as a way to avoid error accumulation and strategic responding is relevant to our design choices. If we use BDM (which is essentially a single response), errors from misunderstanding the mechanism are not compounded across choices, but the mechanism still requires reasoning about all possible prices simultaneously.
- The Bet-Based method's robustness to utility curvature and probability distortions under very weak assumptions (only act monotonicity within a source) contrasts sharply with BDM's vulnerability to comprehension failures despite its weak theoretical IC requirements.

### CS Comments

### Open Questions

- How do the three methods compare specifically for the types of beliefs our project uses (probability of a single event)?
- Could the BB method serve as a benchmark against which to compare BDM-elicited beliefs in our design?

## 7. Dustan, Koutout & Leo (2023) — Reduction in Belief Elicitation

**Citation:** Dustan, A., Koutout, K., & Leo, G. (2023). Reduction in Belief Elicitation. Working Paper. Pre-registered: AEA RCT Registry AEARCTR-0007939.
**Read date:** Mar 29, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Reduction_in_Belief_Elicitation.pdf`

### Summary
Tests whether the *reduction of compound lotteries* assumption -- required for IC of the BQSR but NOT required for MPL/BDM -- is violated empirically and whether violations predict inaccurate belief reports. Also introduces a novel Rank-Ordered Elicitation (ROE) procedure that does not require reduction. The ROE uses multiple price lists where subjects rank a subjective bet among objective lotteries of known probabilities.

### Key Findings

- **70% of participants violate reduction** for at least some of the compound lotteries involved in the BQSR. Only 30% are consistent "reducers" across all relevant lotteries.
- **Reducers are 13 pp more likely to report accurate beliefs** (correctly report the objective probability) than non-reducers in the BQSR. This is the first intra-participant evidence linking reduction failure to belief accuracy.
- **The accuracy gap is attenuated at p=50%:** As theory predicts, the difference between reducers and non-reducers in reporting accuracy disappears when eliciting the centered probability (50%), because all compound lotteries at p=50% have equal overall winning probability (75%), making reduction irrelevant.
- **ROE does NOT improve accuracy** over BQSR, even for non-reducers. The BQSR and ROE perform similarly for both reducers and non-reducers. This is the key surprising finding.
- **Reduction failure alone does not fully explain BIC failure:** Since removing the need for reduction (via ROE) does not improve accuracy, other behavioral factors must also be at play. The paper explicitly calls for expanding selection criteria beyond theoretical IC to include attention, heuristics, and other behavioral factors -- citing Danz et al. (2022) on "behavioral incentive compatibility."

### Method
Two-stage within-subject experiment. Stage 1: subjects choose between pairs of compound lotteries (one of which corresponds to the BQSR lottery at a given belief report) to measure reduction-consistent preferences. Stage 2: subjects report beliefs about objective probabilities using both the BQSR and the ROE. Pre-registered hypotheses (AEA RCT Registry).

### Relevance to Our Project
- **Directly relevant to our framing.** This paper tests one specific explanation for why BQSR fails BIC (reduction) and finds it is part of the story but not the whole story. Our paper tests explanations for why *BDM* fails BIC. Since BDM does NOT require reduction (only statewise monotonicity), our question is complementary: what breaks BDM given its even weaker assumptions?
- **The null ROE result is important:** It shows that fixing the theoretical assumption alone is not enough -- behavioral factors persist even in mechanisms with weaker IC requirements. This supports our approach of investigating behavioral mechanisms (comprehension, preference for control, effort, cognitive competition) rather than just searching for a mechanism with even weaker IC conditions.
- **The "only 30% reduce" finding** provides a useful comparison: if only 30% of people satisfy BQSR's IC assumptions, what fraction satisfy BDM's weaker statewise monotonicity? Our project can address this.
- Speaks most directly to Hypothesis A (comprehension) indirectly -- reduction failure could stem from not understanding the compound lottery structure. But the authors frame it as a preference violation rather than a comprehension issue.

### CS Comments
I need to understand why BDM does not require reduction.

### Open Questions

- If ROE does not help despite removing the reduction requirement, and our project finds BDM also fails BIC despite requiring only statewise monotonicity, what is the common factor? Possibly contingent reasoning / comprehension failure is the bottleneck regardless of the theoretical IC assumption.

## 8. Martin & Munoz-Rodriguez (2022) — Cognitive Costs and Misperceived Incentives: Evidence from the BDM Mechanism

**Citation:** Martin, D. & Munoz-Rodriguez, E. (2022). Cognitive Costs and Misperceived Incentives: Evidence from the BDM Mechanism. *European Economic Review*, 148, 104197. doi:10.1016/j.euroecorev.2022.104197
**Read date:**
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Martin_Munoz-Rodriguez_2022_EER_Cognitive_Costs_BDM.pdf`

### Summary
Studies BDM value elicitation, models misperception of payoff function where subjects could confuse the BDM payoff function with the first price auction(FPA) payoff function.
### Key Findings

- misperceptions of BDM incentives respond to both the costs and benefits of better understanding of the mechanism's incentives
- addressing misperceptions with cost reductions is more robust than with benefit increases for BDM
- Theory shows that whether average bid improves with improvement in the benefits of better perception depends on how mistaken a subject initially was.
  - subjects with an extreme prior belief of being in a FPA would get further from the correct bid
  - moderately mistaken subjects will strictly improve their bidding behavior on average.

### Method
Model + experiment. The experiment adds a "contingent thinking" protocol that frames each possible posted price as a potential bid by a separate computerized player, with payoff tables enumerating all contingencies explicitly. This reduces the perception costs of understanding the BDM's payoff structure. **This is the origin of the CBC (contingency-by-contingency) refinement** — Brown et al. (2026) explicitly adopted Martin & Munoz-Rodriguez's framing for their CBC treatment, citing it as the source (Brown et al. p. 2, 7). The key design idea (reframing the random price as a set of competing computer bidders) originated here.

### Relevance to Our Project
- Their "contingent-thinking" treatment (reframing the BDM as a series of hypothetical opponents at each posted price) is the best existing experimental evidence for Hypothesis A (comprehension) in VALUE BDM. By reducing the cognitive cost of understanding the mechanism -- making the contingent reasoning explicit rather than requiring subjects to do it themselves -- they show that misperceptions respond to how the mechanism is presented, not just to its formal properties.
- The cost-reduction > benefit-increase asymmetry has direct design implications for our project. If we want to test whether comprehension drives BDM BIC failure in beliefs, cost-reduction treatments (e.g., simplifying the explanation, decomposing the mechanism into explicit contingencies, or using the "BDM as hidden MPL" framing from Healy & Leo) are predicted to be more effective than benefit-increase treatments (e.g., raising stakes or providing detailed payoff calculators). This should inform our treatment design.
- Critically, this is value elicitation, not belief elicitation. The translation of their results to beliefs is untested. In value BDM, the "first-price auction" misconception has a clear analogue (shade your bid below your value). In belief BDM, what is the analogous misconception? Subjects might confuse BDM-for-beliefs with a different game form, but which one is not obvious. Identifying the belief-domain analogue of FPA confusion is an open question that our project could address.

## 9. Chakraborty & Kendall (2025) — Uniquely Justifiable Strategies and Mechanism Design

**Citation:** Chakraborty, A. & Kendall, C. (2025). Uniquely Justifiable Strategies and Mechanism Design with Boundedly Rational Players. Working Paper, September 2025. Supersedes "Future Self-Proof Elicitation Mechanisms" (SSRN #4032946, 2022).
**Read date:** Mar 29, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Chakraborty_Kendall_2025_UJS_elicitation.pdf`

### Summary
Introduces the concept of *Uniquely Justifiable Strategy (UJS)* mechanisms -- mechanisms where the dominant strategy is the only action that can be justified as a best response to ANY payoff-relevant contingency (including one's own future actions) at every decision point. Contrasts UJS with OSP (Li 2017a), which requires comparing best/worst case outcomes but still demands contingent reasoning about one's own future actions. Proves that for binary allocation mechanisms (including elicitation), UJS and OSP are mutually exclusive (with 3+ types). Characterizes UJS mechanisms as generalized MPLs. Tests BDM, OSP-E (clock), and UJS-E (clock variant) experimentally.

### Key Findings

- **BDM is NOT a UJS mechanism:** In BDM, the player has the coarsest possible information partition (single decision), so no simplification is possible. But with many possible random prices, many non-dominant bids are justifiable at each contingency (e.g., if random price >= 1.05, any bid below 1.05 is justified). The player must reason through ALL contingencies to see that truthful bidding is optimal.
- **Clock auction (OSP-E) is NOT a UJS mechanism:** The dominated action of exiting early is justifiable if the player considers a contingency where she does not foresee exiting at her valuation. Failing to forecast one's own future actions is sufficient to justify non-optimal current actions.
- **UJS-E (modified clock) IS a UJS mechanism:** Identical to OSP-E except the game only ends when the clock reaches the random price (no early exit option). In a simplified version where histories at the same price are pooled, continuing is the uniquely justifiable action at prices above one's valuation. The dominant strategy emerges without contingent reasoning.
- **UJS mechanisms take the form of a generalized MPL:** A sequence of independent binary decisions (item vs. price), with one randomly selected to determine the outcome. Each decision is independently justified.
- **OSP and UJS are mutually exclusive** in binary allocation with 3+ types (Proposition 2). No mechanism can satisfy both.
- **Experimental results (value elicitation, WTA for $1 item):**
  - Both UJS-E and OSP-E greatly outperform BDM. Contingent reasoning failures play a role in BDM's failures.
  - 60% of subjects do NOT play rationally in OSP-E. Of irrational OSP-E subjects, >70% choose the dominated action ($1.00 exit), which is justifiable only under suboptimal future contingencies.
  - In diagnostic treatment UJ-OSP (which makes the dominated action uniquely justifiable by forcing exit at $1), subjects play the dominated action significantly less often. This confirms OSP failures are tied to justifiability of the dominated action.
  - **OSP-UJS tradeoff:** UJS-E outperforms OSP-E in the natural setting (where OSP-E allows early exit), but when subjects are forced to remain engaged (as in UJS-E), the advantage weakens. OSP mechanisms fail because subjects cannot forecast their own future actions; UJS mechanisms fail because subjects can switch back and forth (multiple switching in the price list).
- **SOSP (Strongly OSP) cannot exist** as an elicitation mechanism with 3+ types (Proposition 1).

### Method
Online experiment. Five treatments: (1) BDM, (2) OSP-E (descending clock, can exit by choosing $1), (3) UJS-E (descending clock, ends at random price), (4) UJ-OSP (diagnostic: OSP-E but forced exit at $1), (5) OSP-L (diagnostic: elicits subjects' conjectured future actions). Subjects elicit WTA for an item worth $v* (induced). Valuations drawn from {$0.05, $0.10, ..., $1.50} in $0.05 increments.

### Relevance to Our Project
- **Central to Hypothesis A (comprehension).** The paper provides a formal theory of WHY BDM is hard to understand: in BDM, many non-dominant actions are *justifiable* because subjects can rationalize any bid by considering a specific contingency (random price realization). The dominant strategy only emerges when subjects reason through ALL contingencies simultaneously. This is a precise formalization of comprehension failure.
- **The OSP-UJS tradeoff is directly relevant to our design:** MPL/price lists are the UJS format for belief elicitation. Our project can frame the choice between BDM and MPL as a choice along the OSP-UJS tradeoff. BDM is neither OSP nor UJS; MPL is UJS but not OSP; clock is OSP but not UJS.
- **The 60% failure rate in OSP-E** and >70% choosing the justifiable-but-dominated action provides strong evidence that contingent reasoning failure (not just "confusion") is the mechanism behind BDM failure. This maps onto our comprehension hypothesis but suggests it is specifically *contingent reasoning* rather than general comprehension.
- **Diagnostic approach is a model for our design:** Their use of UJ-OSP (making the dominated action uniquely justifiable) to diagnose the source of OSP failures suggests we could use analogous diagnostic treatments for BDM -- e.g., decomposing BDM into contingency-by-contingency decisions (as in Brown et al.'s CBC approach) to test whether it is the simultaneous-contingency reasoning that drives failure.

### CS Comments
need to understand the formal theory of comprehension failure.

### Open Questions

- Does the UJS framework apply directly to belief elicitation (probabilistic BDM), not just value elicitation? The paper's experiments use value elicitation; belief elicitation adds the complication of subjective probabilities vs. objective lottery probabilities. Is the MPL for beliefs also UJS in the same sense?
- How does the multiple-switching problem in UJS mechanisms (price lists) trade off against the contingent-reasoning problem in BDM? Which is quantitatively worse for belief elicitation accuracy?

## 10. Benoit, Dubra & Romagnoli (2022) — Belief Elicitation When More Than Money Matters: Controlling for "Control"

**Citation:** Benoit, J.-P., Dubra, J., & Romagnoli, G. (2022). Belief Elicitation When More Than Money Matters: Controlling for "Control." *American Economic Journal: Microeconomics*, 14(3), 837-888. doi:10.1257/mic.20200017
**Read date:** Mar 29, 2026 (librarian)
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/benoit_dubra_romagnoli_2022_control.pdf`

### Summary
Investigates whether nonmonetary motives distort belief reports in the widely used matching probabilities method. The paper identifies TWO distinct nonmonetary concerns: (i) **"preference for control"** (desire to bet on outcomes contingent on one's own performance rather than on equivalent random devices), and (ii) **"positive statements"** (desire to make positive statements about oneself, either for self-regard or to induce favorable treatment from the experimenter). The experiments primarily test the control channel (a probabilistic BDM in the Karni 2009 family). Two experiments. Experiment 1: subjects report beliefs about their probability of being in the top half on a quiz, using two mechanisms -- (i) the standard matching probabilities method (bet on own performance vs. objective lottery) and (ii) a novel control-mitigating design (bet on own performance on one task vs. own performance on another task). Experiment 2: a series of treatments where subjects sometimes bet on doing well and sometimes bet on doing badly, to decompose the control motive into a "desire to bet on doing well" and an "anti-control" motive for doing badly.

### Key Findings

- Under the standard matching probabilities method, subjects inflate reported beliefs by **18 percentage points** relative to the control-mitigating mechanism (average reported chance of being in the top half is 54% under the standard method vs. a benchmark closer to 50% in an unbiased population). Both mechanisms are incentive compatible in money -- the difference must come from nonmonetary concerns.
- At least **68% of what is normally measured as "overconfidence"** in the matching probabilities paradigm is actually preference for control, not genuine belief distortion.
- The control motivation is **asymmetric**: it manifests only as a desire to bet on doing *well* on a task. Betting on doing *badly* is perceived as a negative -- subjects exhibit an "anti-control" motive, preferring objective lotteries over bets on their own poor performance. This means control is not a general preference for agency but a preference for positive-outcome agency.
- The control-mitigating design (bet on self in both arms) can be adapted to various elicitation mechanisms, including the binarized scoring rule and the randomized scoring rule.

### Method
Two lab experiments. Experiment 1: within-subject comparison of standard matching probabilities vs. control-mitigating mechanism for self-beliefs about quiz performance. Experiment 2: between-subject treatments varying whether subjects bet on doing well vs. doing badly to decompose the control motive. Both experiments use the Karni (2009) probabilistic BDM family.

### Relevance to Our Project
- This is the strongest existing evidence for Hypothesis B (preference for control as a driver of BDM belief misreporting). The 18pp effect size is enormous and the identification is clean: both mechanisms are IC in money, so the gap must come from nonmonetary concerns.
- The asymmetry finding (control = desire to bet on doing well; anti-control for doing badly) predicts that BDM misreporting should be *directional* in self-belief settings: inflation of beliefs about positive outcomes, deflation of beliefs about negative outcomes. This is testable in our design.
- Their control-mitigating design (bet on self in both arms) suggests a concrete treatment arm for our experiment: if we replace the objective lottery in BDM with a subjective event that the participant also controls, and misreporting decreases, we have evidence for control as a driver of BIC failure.
- **Critical limitation for our project:** Their context is confidence elicitation -- beliefs about one's OWN performance. In our induced-probability urn-draw design, the event is NOT under the subject's control (it is an objective random draw). Preference for control should therefore be absent or greatly attenuated in our setting. This means their results predict that Hypothesis B should NOT explain BIC failure in our urn-draw paradigm -- which is itself an informative test. If BDM still fails BIC in urn draws (where control is irrelevant), the failure must be driven by other channels (comprehension, effort, cognitive competition).

### CS Comments
- In the Formalism section (p. 843-844), under "Two Important Mechanisms," they claim the optimal report under matching probabilities simplifies to p* = μ + c_H·μ + n. Working through the FOC of their expected utility function U(p) = [pμ + (1-p²)/2]·[u(w+m) - u(w)] + u(w) + c_H·pμ + n·p, the derivative gives (μ - p)·Δu + c_H·μ + n = 0, which solves to p* = μ + (c_H·μ + n)/Δu. The utility terms u(w+m) and u(w) do NOT cancel — Δu = u(w+m) - u(w) remains in the denominator. Their simplified expression p* = μ + c_H·μ + n requires an implicit normalization Δu = 1, which is valid since utility is unique up to affine transformations under EU, but this normalization is never stated. With Δu = 1, the FOC does give p* = μ + c_H·μ + n, confirming the expression. This doesn't affect their experimental results (which don't depend on this formula) but the unstated normalization is worth noting.
- They also attach complete experimental instructions. May be useful.

### Open Questions
- Does preference for control operate in urn-draw settings where the event is objective and not under the subject's control? Their Section II.A ("Beliefs about Objective Events") acknowledges this is possible but provides no direct evidence.

## 11. Burfurd & Wilkening (2022) — Cognitive Heterogeneity and Complex Belief Elicitation

**Citation:** Burfurd, I. & Wilkening, T. (2022). Cognitive Heterogeneity and Complex Belief Elicitation. *Experimental Economics*, 25, 557-592. doi:10.1007/s10683-021-09722-x
**Read date:** Mar 29, 2026 (librarian)
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/burfurd_wilkening_2021.pdf`

### Summary
Uses a two-part design to study the interaction between the Stochastic BDM (SBDM) mechanism, task complexity, and cognitive heterogeneity in belief elicitation. Part 1: a "Bucket Game" (variant of Charness & Levin 2005 and Charness et al. 2007) classifies participants as "consistent" or "inconsistent" with probabilistic reasoning based on whether their choices respect stochastic dominance and Bayesian updating. In each period, participants are assigned one of two buckets (equal probability), draw a ball from the left side, observe its color, then decide whether to draw their second ball from the left or right side of their bucket -- where Bayes' rule dictates switching after a success and staying after a failure. Inconsistent participants violate this pattern (e.g., using a reinforcement-learning heuristic of "stay after success"). Part 2: participants elicit beliefs using either SBDM or Introspection (unincentivized report) for both easy decision problems (uninformative signals where the correct belief is trivially 50%) and hard decision problems (informative signals requiring Bayesian updating).

### Key Findings

- **SBDM reduces sensitivity to task difficulty:** In the SBDM mechanism, the mean error of participants in easy decision problems is 20.7% smaller than in hard problems. In Introspection, the corresponding figure is 53.8%. The difference in sensitivities is significant in a permutation test. This suggests SBDM encourages more careful thinking.
- **SBDM amplifies heterogeneity in probabilistic reasoning:** The mean error of a consistent participant is 37.6% smaller than an inconsistent participant under SBDM, but only 22.4% smaller under Introspection. The difference in sensitivities is significant. SBDM widens the gap between good and poor probabilistic reasoners.
- **No significant interaction between mechanism and cognitive ability or effort:** In a follow-up experiment measuring fluid intelligence (Raven's Progressive Matrices) and cognitive effort (extended CRT), both measures strongly predict errors under BOTH mechanisms, but there is no significant interaction effect between elicitation mechanism and either ability or effort. The relevant cognitive dimension is specifically probabilistic reasoning (as measured by the Bucket Game), not general intelligence or effort.
- **The interaction effect is strongest for easy problems** where the correct belief is obvious and differences in belief errors are most likely driven by mechanism confusion rather than incorrect Bayesian updating. This is consistent with the interpretation that SBDM's complexity is the source of the differential, not the difficulty of forming the underlying belief.
- **Caveat:** The interaction effect was large and significant in the initial lab experiments but small and non-significant in online follow-up experiments. The authors note value in future replications.

### Method
Two-part within-subject design. Part 1: Bucket Game (20 rounds) to classify consistent/inconsistent participants. Part 2: belief elicitation under SBDM vs. Introspection (between-subject) for easy and hard Bayesian updating problems. The Bucket Game uses an urn task (two buckets, each with two sides of 20 balls, draw-and-replace). Belief elicitation varies the composition of balls in the bucket and the number of draws to create easy (uninformative) and hard (informative) problems. Initial lab experiment at University of Melbourne; pre-registered follow-up experiment online. Data pooled per pre-analysis plan.

### Relevance to Our Project
- Directly relevant to Hypothesis D (cognitive competition between the belief-formation task and the mechanism comprehension task). Their finding that SBDM amplifies heterogeneity in probabilistic reasoning -- but NOT in general cognitive ability or effort -- suggests that the binding cognitive constraint for BDM-type mechanisms is specifically the ability to reason probabilistically about the mechanism's incentive structure, not general intelligence. This refines what "cognitive competition" means: it is not that BDM competes for generic cognitive resources, but that it specifically competes for probabilistic reasoning capacity.
- The null result on the mechanism x ability interaction complicates the simple "cognitive resource competition" story. If more able subjects (as measured by Raven's and CRT) do not differentially benefit from SBDM, then SBDM's cognitive demands may not be about raw processing power. Instead, the relevant skill is specifically understanding how probabilistic mechanisms work -- a form of "mechanism literacy" that is captured by the Bucket Game but not by standard cognitive ability measures.
- Their Bucket Game design is a model for how to measure probabilistic reasoning consistency in our experiment. We could use a similar classification task to identify subjects who are likely to struggle with BDM's probabilistic structure, and then test whether BIC failure is concentrated among these inconsistent reasoners.
- The easy-problem result (where the correct belief is trivially 50% and differences must come from mechanism confusion) is particularly useful for our induced-probability design, where we can similarly construct tasks where the belief is known and errors must be attributed to the mechanism rather than to belief-formation difficulty.

## 12. Gonzalez-Fernandez, Bosch-Rosa & Meissner (2025) — Direct Elicitation of Parametric Belief Distributions

**Citation:** Gonzalez-Fernandez, P., Bosch-Rosa, C., & Meissner, T. (2025). Direct Elicitation of Parametric Belief Distributions: An Application to Inflation Expectations. *Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization*, 233, 106947. doi:10.1016/j.jebo.2025.106947
**Read date:** Mar 29, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Gonzalez-Fernandez_Bosch-Rosa_Meissner_2025.pdf`

### Summary
Introduces a novel "Direct" method for eliciting full belief distributions (parameterized as beta distributions) and benchmarks it against the standard "Bins" method used by the New York Fed's Survey of Consumer Expectations (SCE). The Direct method works in two steps: (1) respondents specify their minimum and maximum expected inflation rates, then (2) they use two sliders -- one for the mean, one for the variance -- to shape a beta distribution displayed graphically in real time. This contrasts with the Bins method, where respondents allocate probabilities across predefined intervals and researchers fit a beta distribution ex post. The paper conducts a pre-registered online survey (N=939 after exclusions) on Prolific with a US representative sample, eliciting 12-month-ahead inflation expectations. All respondents completed both the Direct method and one of two bins methods (Original or Adaptive), with treatment and order randomized.

### Key Findings

- **Elicitation method significantly affects the resulting belief distribution.** The Direct method produces higher mean inflation expectations (5.55%) than the Original bins method (3.75%) or the Adaptive bins method (5.93%), with the Direct-Original difference highly significant (p < 0.001). This suggests the Original bins method's predefined intervals centered around zero artificially pull beliefs downward.
- **The Direct method produces tighter distributions.** Standard deviations are substantially lower under the Direct method (0.78) than under either the Original (3.49) or Adaptive (1.36) bins methods (all p < 0.001). The ex post fitting in the bins methods appears to inflate uncertainty.
- **Respondents prefer the Direct method.** On Likert-scale comparisons, respondents rate the Direct method as significantly easier to use (mean 3.45 vs. 4 on 7-point scale, p < 0.001), more engaging (mean 2.45 vs. 4, p < 0.001), and better at expressing their beliefs (mean 3.79 vs. 4, p = 0.062 for Original; 3.37 vs. 4, p < 0.001 for Adaptive).
- **The Direct method better aligns with respondents' own beliefs.** In tailored inflation scenarios (probability of deflation, median, probability of inflation exceeding 5%), respondents rated the Direct-method-derived scenarios as significantly closer to their beliefs than Original-bins-derived scenarios for two of three measures (p < 0.001).
- **The Direct method better predicts point beliefs.** OLS regressions of point inflation predictions on distribution means yield R-squared = 0.564 for Direct vs. 0.181 for Original and 0.426 for Adaptive. The coefficient for Direct (0.743) is closer to 1 than for either bins method.
- **The Original bins method generates artifactual deflationary expectations** -- a substantial share of respondents show mean beliefs below zero, which the authors argue reflects the method's predefined intervals rather than genuine beliefs.
- **Completion time is faster for the Direct method** (52.5s vs. 87.4s for Original, 105.9s for Adaptive; all p < 0.001), though when instruction-reading time is included, total time is longer for Direct (165.7s) because only Direct required on-screen instructions.

### Method
Pre-registered online survey experiment on Prolific (US representative sample). N=968 recruited, N=939 retained after pre-registered exclusions (minimum-maximum spread < 0.5pp, point beliefs outside [-20%, 50%]). Within-subject comparison: all respondents completed both the Direct method and one of two bins methods. Between-subject randomization into Direct + Original (N=469) or Direct + Adaptive (N=470), with order also randomized. Flat payment of 1.38 GBP (no incentive compatibility for belief reports -- the elicitation is unincentivized). Median completion time 10:38 minutes. Validation via three types of measures: direct preference comparisons, alignment with tailored inflation scenarios, and correlation with point predictions and planned consumption.

### Relevance to Our Project
- **Alternative to BDM for full distribution elicitation.** The Direct method sidesteps BDM entirely by having respondents directly construct a parametric distribution via sliders rather than reporting a single probability that is then used in a lottery mechanism. This is relevant as a methodological contrast: while our project studies WHY BDM fails behaviorally, this paper offers one possible "what instead" -- a non-incentivized but cognitively simpler approach to belief elicitation that respondents prefer and that produces distributions better aligned with their stated beliefs.
- **Supports the comprehension channel (Hypothesis A).** The finding that respondents prefer the Direct method and produce more internally consistent beliefs with it suggests that the cognitive burden of the Bins method (and by extension, BDM-type mechanisms that require understanding lottery structures) is a real barrier. The Direct method works precisely because it requires no understanding of probability lotteries or incentive compatibility -- respondents simply shape a curve. This aligns with the prediction that reducing cognitive demands improves belief reporting.
- **Unincentivized elicitation performs well.** The Direct method is entirely unincentivized, yet produces beliefs that are more predictive of point estimates and better aligned with respondents' own stated beliefs than the incentive-compatible (in principle) bins method. This is consistent with the broader finding in the literature (see also Grapow 2026, Paper 5) that unincentivized methods can match or outperform incentivized ones, which raises the question: if the incentive structure of BDM adds cognitive cost without reliably improving accuracy, what exactly does BDM buy?
- **The method is NOT incentive-compatible and does not use BDM.** This is a critical distinction from our project. The Direct method cannot be used in settings where IC is required (e.g., laboratory experiments where incentive alignment is the norm). Our project's contribution -- understanding why BDM fails BIC and how to fix it -- remains relevant precisely for those settings. The Direct method is a complement (for surveys) rather than a substitute (for experiments).
- **Partition dependence and framing effects.** The paper's finding that the Original bins method generates artifactual deflationary beliefs due to predefined intervals centered at zero is a concrete example of how the FORMAT of elicitation (not just its IC properties) shapes reported beliefs. This reinforces the motivation for our project: even within IC mechanisms, the presentation format (BDM vs. MPL vs. slider) may matter as much as the theoretical properties.

## 13. Burfurd & Wilkening (2018) — Experimental Guidance for Eliciting Beliefs with the SBDM

**Citation:** Burfurd, I. & Wilkening, T. (2018). Experimental Guidance for Eliciting Beliefs with the Stochastic Becker-DeGroot-Marschak Mechanism. *Journal of the Economic Science Association*, 4, 57-78. (Working paper version: November 2017.)
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Burfurd_and_Wilkening_2018_JESA.pdf`

### Summary
Compares three isomorphic presentation formats of the SBDM and studies the effect of comprehension quizzes and delivery method (electronic vs. paper) on belief elicitation quality. Uses induced-probability Bayesian updating tasks (urn draws with known posteriors of 33% and 67%) to measure accuracy against ground truth.

### Key Findings

- **Three SBDM formats compared (Experiment 1, N=125):**
  1. **Holt-Smith (HS):** Rigorous descriptive instructions, 936 words, 6 screens, direct numeric entry
  2. **Hao-Houser (HH):** Analogy-based ("chips-in-a-bag"), 397 words, 2 screens, direct numeric entry
  3. **Trautmann-van de Kuilen (TK):** List-style two-step titration, 391 words, 4 screens

- **Accuracy and precision do NOT differ across formats** (KW p=0.546 for accuracy, p=0.821 for precision). Mean absolute errors: HS 12.4, HH 13.1, TK 14.7.
- **HH is significantly faster** (850s vs. 1089s HS, 1212s TK; KW p<0.001). Nearly 3 minutes faster on instructions alone (305s vs. 480s).
- **TK is slowest per period** (40s vs. 22-26s) due to two-step titration interface. TK also produces 17% "reverse" reports (reporting 33 after dark ball, i.e., wrong posterior direction), suggesting subjects lose track of which signal they observed.

- **Dropping the comprehension quiz nearly doubles error (Experiment 2, N=88):**
  - Quiz (Q): mean absolute error 11.2; No Quiz (NQ): 20.4; Paper (P): 13.4 (KW p=0.042)
  - **Boundary reports (0 or 100) explode without quiz:** 26.2% of all NQ observations, vs. <6% with quiz
  - NQ subjects initially make boundary reports then learn over time — the quiz provides critical initial understanding
  - Electronic vs. paper delivery does NOT affect accuracy (Q vs. P: p=0.230)

- **Recommendation:** Use HH (analogy-based) format for speed; always include a comprehension quiz; electronic delivery is fine.

### Method
Experiment 1: University of Melbourne, July 2015, N=125 (42 HS, 41 HH, 42 TK). Bayesian updating task: 2 buckets (Bucket A: 2 dark + 1 light; Bucket B: 2 light + 1 dark), one randomly chosen, one ball drawn, subjects report belief Bucket A was chosen. True posteriors: 67% (dark ball) or 33% (light ball). 15 periods per subject. Payment: $15 show-up + $15 or $0 from one random period (avg $25.95).

Experiment 2: Same lab, N=88. Three treatments using HH format only: Quiz (n=28), No Quiz (n=30), Paper (n=30). Same task.

Robustness check: Spinner task (N=102, no Bayesian updating) replicates the null format result.

### Relevance to Our Project
- **Direct evidence that implementation matters for behavioral IC.** The SBDM is theoretically IC, but dropping the comprehension quiz nearly doubles mean error and causes 26% boundary reports. This is strong evidence that BIC failure is partly a comprehension/scaffolding failure, not purely a preference failure.
- **Comprehension quizzes are a necessary (but not sufficient) condition for BIC.** Without quizzes, subjects do not understand the mechanism well enough to play optimally. With quizzes, accuracy is reasonable but still far from perfect (mean abs error 11-13pp).
- **Format null result is informative:** Once subjects receive adequate training (all had quizzes in Exp 1), the specific framing (descriptive vs. analogy vs. list) matters less than whether subjects understand the core mechanism. This suggests the binding constraint is comprehension of the mechanism's incentive logic, not the surface presentation.
- **Connection to Burfurd & Wilkening 2022:** The 2018 paper establishes HH as the recommended format and shows quizzes are essential. The 2022 paper then uses this implementation to show that even with good implementation, individual differences in probabilistic reasoning drive remaining BIC failures. Together: implementation matters (2018) → cognitive heterogeneity matters given good implementation (2022).
- **Boundary reports as a BIC failure diagnostic:** 26% boundary rates without quizzes vs. <6% with quizzes provides a clean measure of comprehension-driven BIC failure in induced-probability settings.

### Instruction Texts (from Online Appendix C of the supplement)
The supplement includes full verbatim instructions and quizzes for all three treatments. Key differences:

**HS (Holt-Smith) instructions** explain the mechanism in full formal detail: subject reports P (0-100), computer draws N (0-100, "Dice Lottery"), then two cases are enumerated: "Case of N less than P" (reject Dice Lottery, play A Lottery) and "Case of N greater than P" (accept Dice Lottery). Then states the equivalence: "the chances of earning the $15 prize are P out of 100, and this is the sense in which they are equivalent." Long, rigorous, but dense — 936 words.

**HH (Hao-Houser) instructions** use a concrete "Lottery Bag" analogy: the computer creates a bag with 100 chips, "?" of which are black and the rest white. The unknown "?" corresponds to the random draw. Key framing: "Based on your reported belief... the computer will select the game that gives you a higher chance of winning $15." Summary is just 4 sentences. Very short — 397 words.

**TK (Trautmann-van de Kuilen) instructions** present a list of choices between "Bucket Game" and "Lottery Game" with a table showing the tradeoff at each probability level. Two-stage titration: Stage 1 picks a 10-point range, Stage 2 refines within it. Visual but requires understanding list format — 391 words, 4 screens.

**Quiz structure** is similar across treatments (6 questions in HS; adapted versions in HH and TK). The HS quiz includes an "error scenario" (you typed 18 by mistake when you believe 81) that tests whether subjects understand the consequences of misreporting. The TK quiz adapts this to list format (asks about switching points and which game to prefer).

### CS Comments
- The TK "reverse report" finding (17% reporting wrong posterior direction) is a cautionary tale for list-style mechanisms — subjects can be confused about the STATE, not just the mechanism. In our design with induced probabilities, this should be less of an issue since we can simplify the belief-formation task (e.g., urn composition stated directly).
- Their HH format is essentially what we should use if we implement SBDM — short, analogy-based, with a comprehension quiz. But the quiz finding also suggests our BIC test should compare with-quiz vs. without-quiz conditions, or at minimum always include a quiz and measure quiz performance as a covariate.
- Having now read all three instruction sets: the HH instructions are strikingly simpler. The "Lottery Bag" analogy (bag with "?" black chips out of 100) makes the random draw concrete without requiring subjects to reason through two cases. The HS instructions, by contrast, require subjects to process two separate cases (N < P and N >= P) and understand why the mechanism selects "whichever is better for you." The TK instructions avoid this by replacing the single-number report with a list of explicit comparisons, but at the cost of requiring subjects to understand the list format and two-stage titration process.

### Open Questions
- Does the quiz effect persist across many rounds, or does it only matter for the first few periods? Their data shows NQ subjects learn over time — at what point does the gap close?
- Would even more intensive training (e.g., practice rounds with feedback, worked examples) further reduce BIC failures beyond what the quiz achieves?

## 14. Hao & Houser (2012) — Belief Elicitation in the Presence of Naive Respondents

**Citation:** Hao, L. & Houser, D. (2012). Belief Elicitation in the Presence of Naive Respondents: An Experimental Study. *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty*, 44, 161-180. doi:10.1007/s11166-011-9133-1
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Hao_Hauser_2011_JRU.pdf` (note: filename has author name typo — should be Houser)

### Summary
Compares two belief elicitation mechanisms from Karni (2009) — a "declarative" mechanism (essentially probabilistic BDM) and a "clock" mechanism (ascending clock auction) — when the population includes both sophisticated and naive participants. Both mechanisms share the same theoretical IC conditions (dominance + probabilistic sophistication), but the clock has a structural advantage: it censors naive responses that would otherwise be recorded as errors. Uses induced (objective) beliefs of 0.2 and 0.3 with physical props (bags of chips).

### Key Findings

- **Fewer than half (47%) of novice responses are optimal in the declarative/BDM mechanism.** 53% non-optimal even with: objective beliefs, physical props (bags, chips), read-aloud instructions, comprehension quizzes, no probabilistic language.
- **Clock mechanism outperforms declarative among novices**, but the numbers require careful interpretation:
  - Among ALL clock observations: 39% optimal, 22% non-optimal, 39% censored (dummy bidder exits before subject acts)
  - Among NON-CENSORED clock observations: 64% optimal (30 out of 47 non-censored)
  - Declarative: 47% optimal, 53% non-optimal (no censoring)
  - WMW test: p=0.096 two-sided (p<0.05 one-sided, per footnote 27)
  - The advantage comes from two channels:
    1. **Censoring:** 39% of clock observations are automatically filtered (naive subjects who didn't act before the dummy bidder exited)
    2. **Better strategy adoption:** Even among non-censored observations, clock responses show higher dominant strategy play (64% vs. 47%). The sequential format makes the dominant strategy easier to identify.
- **The clock distribution more accurately characterizes true beliefs** even after filtering declarative data — the filtered declarative distribution has its mode at 0.1 (far from true values of 0.2/0.3), while the clock has its mode at 0.3.
- **Experience partially closes the gap:** After one round, accuracy is 57% (declarative) vs. 60% (clock) among all observations — not significantly different (p=0.84 two-sided). But distributional differences persist (p=0.052).
- **Comprehension quiz passage does NOT guarantee strategic comprehension.** Most subjects answered quiz correctly but still played non-optimally in the declarative mechanism. This is an important distinction: understanding the procedure ≠ understanding the dominant strategy.
- **Theoretical result:** The clock's accuracy advantage holds specifically when true beliefs are below 0.5 (Condition 3 in the paper). This guided their choice of induced beliefs at 0.2 and 0.3.

### Method
Lab experiment at George Mason University (ICES), April-October 2009. N=130 undergraduates. 2×2 within-subject design crossing mechanism order (D+D, D+C, C+D, C+C). Physical props: opaque bags with known (Bag A) and unknown (Bag B) chip compositions. Declarative: report integer 1-9 as switch threshold. Clock: ascending clock with 5-second increments against computerized dummy bidder. Payment: $5 show-up + task earnings (white chip = $10, black chip = $1); average total $16. Round 2 was a surprise.

### Relevance to Our Project
- **The "declarative mechanism" IS the probabilistic BDM for beliefs.** Subject states a number, it's compared to a random draw, payoff depends on which is higher. Structurally identical to our mechanism of interest.
- **Strongest evidence for comprehension failure in BDM-for-beliefs.** 53% non-optimal under near-ideal conditions (known beliefs, physical props, quizzes, no probability language). If BDM fails BIC here, it will certainly fail in more complex settings (subjective beliefs, online, probabilistic framing).
- **Comprehension quizzes are necessary but insufficient.** Subjects pass quizzes but still don't play optimally — they understand the *procedure* but not the *strategy*. This is a more nuanced version of the comprehension hypothesis: the bottleneck is not understanding "how the mechanism works" but understanding "what I should do to maximize my payoff."
- **The clock as a sequential decomposition of BDM:** The clock effectively asks "do you want to switch now?" at each step, converting the single complex decision (declare a number) into a sequence of simple binary decisions. This is analogous to the CBC decomposition in Brown et al. (2026) and connects to the UJS characterization in Chakraborty & Kendall (2025).
- **Experience helps but is not a complete fix.** One round closes the accuracy gap but not the distributional gap. This suggests that even experienced subjects retain some strategic confusion.

### Instruction Texts (from Appendix, pp. 176-178)
The paper includes full verbatim instructions for both mechanisms with visual flowchart diagrams.

**Declarative mechanism instructions** are remarkably concise. Overview: "The procedure is simple. You will first submit a number, and then you will draw a chip from one of two bags." Then: "Bag A has **2** white chips and **8** black chips for a total of **10**. Bag B also has **10** chips, some white, some black, but you do not know how many of each." Core rule: "If the number you submit is less than or equal to *the number of white chips in Bag B*, you will draw from **Bag B**, otherwise you will draw from **Bag A**." Includes a clear flowchart showing the two branches. No probabilistic language. Physical props (sealed envelope with Bag B composition drawn from deck of 9 cards). Very clean design.

**Clock mechanism instructions:** "The computer screen in front of you will start counting from number **1**, and increase by **1** every 5 seconds until it reaches the number in the sealed envelope. You can stop the counting at any point by pressing the space key." Includes matching flowchart (press space → draw from Bag B; don't press → draw from Bag A).

Key design choice: no probability language at all — everything is framed as "number of white chips" and physical card draws. This is the simplest framing I've seen for the Karni mechanisms.

### CS Comments
- Note: filename says "Hao_Hauser" but the actual second author is Daniel Houser (George Mason). The paper is sometimes cited as Hao & Houser (2012) in the literature.
- The induced-probability design (objective beliefs of 0.2 and 0.3) is very close to what we use — our urn-draw paradigm similarly induces known probabilities. Their finding that BDM fails even with known probabilities is directly relevant: the failure is NOT about subjective belief uncertainty but about mechanism comprehension.
- The theoretical result that the clock advantage is concentrated at beliefs < 0.5 is relevant to our parameter choice. If we want to maximize the likelihood of detecting BIC failure, we should use induced probabilities away from 0.5 (where incentives are weakest for all mechanisms).
- This paper is the source of the "HH" (Hao-Houser) format used and recommended by Burfurd & Wilkening (2018). The chips-in-a-bag analogy originated here.
- Having read the actual instructions: Hao & Houser's declarative mechanism instructions are the cleanest BDM-for-beliefs instructions I've seen. No probability language, no lottery terminology, just "submit a number" and "draw from one of two bags." The flowchart is excellent. Even so, 53% of novices play non-optimally — which is a strong indictment of the mechanism itself, not the instructions.
- The discrete state space (integers 1-9 for Bag B composition) creates an issue: there are TWO equivalent dominant strategies (report 2 or 3 when belief = 0.2; report 3 or 4 when belief = 0.3). This ambiguity complicates accuracy measurement. Our design should use a continuous or finer grid to avoid this.

### Open Questions
- The clock's censoring advantage is a double-edged sword: it improves data quality but loses information about naive subjects' beliefs. For our project, which studies WHY BDM fails, we need to observe the errors, not censor them. The declarative/BDM format is actually more informative for diagnosing BIC failures.
- Can the clock's strategy-adoption advantage be captured without the censoring cost? E.g., a "simulated clock" that presents sequential binary choices but records all responses.

## 15. Burdea & Woon (2022) — Online Belief Elicitation Methods

**Citation:** Burdea, V. & Woon, J. (2022). Online Belief Elicitation Methods. *Journal of Economic Psychology*, 90, 102496. doi:10.1016/j.joep.2022.102496
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Burdea_Woon_2022_online_belief_elicitation.pdf`

### Summary
Compares three belief elicitation conditions in an online (MTurk) setting: FLAT (unincentivized, $0.20/part), BDM (stochastic BDM per Mobius et al. 2011), and BSR (binarized scoring rule per Wilson & Vespa 2017). Uses both induced-probability beliefs (objective events with known probabilities: 100, 0, 50, 25, 80) and subjective beliefs (factual confidence, second-order political beliefs). Measures comprehension, perceived difficulty, accuracy, and the role of cognitive factors.

### Key Findings

- **BDM has the worst comprehension:** First-attempt quiz correct rates: FLAT 72%, BSR 73%, **BDM 47%**. Self-reported difficulty: **BDM 3.60** > BSR 2.96 > FLAT 2.43 (all p<0.01). Self-reported effort: **BDM 3.76** > BSR 3.43 > FLAT 2.83.
- **BDM does NOT improve accuracy over flat fee for induced beliefs:** Accuracy (belief = objective probability): FLAT 59.5%, BDM 53.6%, BSR 61.6%. Mean absolute error: FLAT 11.3pp, BDM 12.8pp, BSR 11.3pp. **No significant differences** — and BDM is numerically worst.
- **BSR most effectively moves beliefs off 50%:** BSR has significantly fewer 50% beliefs than FLAT or BDM for both first-order and second-order beliefs. BDM reduces 50% beliefs relative to FLAT but not consistently significantly.
- **Cognitive factors dominate mechanism choice:** Low numeracy: **−42.9pp accuracy** (the largest effect). Failed attention check: −14.2pp. No college degree: −9.8pp. These individual-level factors swamp the treatment effects.
- **IC mechanisms help less-educated subjects:** Significant positive interaction of BDM × no college and BSR × no college — IC mechanisms specifically benefit participants who would otherwise perform worst.
- **Incentivized beliefs are LESS accurate than unincentivized numeracy items with identical probabilities:** 12-16pp accuracy gap (within-subject), suggesting the incentive mechanism may add cognitive cost that offsets its theoretical benefit.

### Method
MTurk, December 2019. N=470 (155 FLAT, 157 BDM, 158 BSR). Between-subjects. Qualtrics survey with slider (0-100) for all conditions. Five belief tasks: induced probabilities, first-order factual confidence, second-order political beliefs (unconditional, conditional on Democrats, conditional on Republicans). Payment: $1.50 fixed + variable bonus (avg $1.17). Average 14 minutes, $11.44/hr. Pre-registered power analysis: d=0.5, 80% power → 150/condition.

Key design choice: same slider format across all conditions (NOT MPL for BDM), isolating the incentive mechanism from the elicitation format. Subjects were told truthful reporting maximizes payment but were NOT shown IC proofs or formulas.

### Relevance to Our Project
- **BDM comprehension is worst of all mechanisms tested.** 47% first-attempt quiz accuracy vs. 73% for BSR — the mechanism that requires the *weakest* theoretical IC assumptions is the *hardest* to understand. This is the core paradox our project addresses.
- **BDM fails to improve accuracy over no incentives.** Despite theoretical IC, BDM-elicited beliefs are not more accurate than flat-fee beliefs. If anything, BDM accuracy is numerically lowest. This is direct evidence of BIC failure in an online setting with induced probabilities.
- **Supports the "cognitive competition" hypothesis (Hypothesis D).** The authors propose a "cognitive production" framework: subjects must exert effort to discover their beliefs, and IC mechanisms increase the incentive for this effort. But BDM's comprehension difficulty increases the cognitive cost of understanding the mechanism, consuming resources that could otherwise go to belief formation. BSR is easier to understand → lower marginal cost of cognitive effort → more effort on belief formation → better data quality. The net effect: BDM's theoretical IC advantage is consumed by its comprehension disadvantage.
- **The 50% belief result is important.** If 50% beliefs represent "I haven't thought about it" rather than genuine uncertainty, then mechanisms that move beliefs off 50% (BSR > BDM > FLAT) are doing useful cognitive work. BDM moves beliefs off 50% less effectively than BSR, consistent with BDM being harder to understand.
- **Online setting with diverse population.** Unlike lab studies with undergrads, this MTurk sample includes varied education, numeracy, and attention levels. The finding that cognitive factors dominate mechanism choice suggests BIC failures will be even worse in representative populations than in student samples.
- **Practical implication:** If BDM does not outperform flat-fee elicitation on accuracy, the case for using BDM rests entirely on its theoretical properties — which this paper and ours show do not translate to behavior.

### CS Comments
- This is the cleanest head-to-head comparison of BDM vs. BSR vs. flat fee for belief elicitation I've seen. The induced-probability task is particularly useful because it provides ground truth.
- The finding that incentivized beliefs are *less* accurate than unincentivized numeracy items is striking and connects to Healy & Leo's recommendation to NOT show incentive details — the mechanism itself may be the problem, not just how it's explained.
- Their BDM implementation uses a slider (not MPL), which means the BDM comprehension results are about the *incentive logic*, not the *elicitation format*. This is exactly the comparison we want: holding format constant, does BDM's incentive structure create additional cognitive cost?
- The interaction result (IC helps less-educated subjects) is nuanced — it means BDM is not useless, just that its benefits are concentrated among subjects who would otherwise be most inaccurate, and these benefits are offset by comprehension costs for the broader population.
- Important design choice: they deliberately did NOT explain formal IC properties — just stated "you have the best chance of winning the bonus when you state your beliefs as accurately as possible." This is the minimal-explanation approach. Their BDM instructions follow Mobius et al. (2011) and Hill (2017): state belief B, computer draws W, if B >= W get paid based on truth, if B < W enter lottery with W% chance. This is a different framing from the Hao-Houser "bag of chips" analogy or the Burfurd-Wilkening "Lottery Bag" — it uses explicit probability language, which may contribute to the higher difficulty rating.
- The 47% BDM comprehension rate (vs 73% BSR, 72% FLAT) is an underappreciated finding. FLAT and BSR have similar quiz scores, but BDM is dramatically worse. This means the *incentive logic of BDM specifically* — not just "having an incentive scheme" — is what creates comprehension difficulty. BSR is just as complex to incentivize but much easier to understand.

### Open Questions
- Would the results change with a more intensive comprehension intervention (e.g., worked examples, practice rounds with feedback, as in Burfurd & Wilkening 2018)?
- Does the BDM comprehension disadvantage persist when using MPL format instead of slider? (Their design holds format constant, but in practice BDM is often implemented as MPL, which might improve comprehension.)
- The within-subject comparison of incentivized vs. unincentivized accuracy is confounded by task order. A between-subject design comparing identical items under BDM vs. flat fee would be cleaner.
- What specific BDM instructions did they use? The paper cites Mobius et al. (2011) and Hill (2017) as sources for their BDM implementation. We should look up these instruction sets to compare with the Hao-Houser and Burfurd-Wilkening versions.

## 16. Healy (2020) — Explaining the BDM — or Any Random Binary Choice Elicitation Mechanism — to Subjects

**Citation:** Healy, P.J. (2020). Explaining the BDM — or Any Random Binary Choice Elicitation Mechanism — to Subjects. Unpublished note, Ohio State University. January 8, 2020.
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Healy-ExplainingBDM_Note.pdf`

### Summary
Pedagogical note arguing that the BDM should be explained to subjects as a list of binary choices rather than as an auction or contingent payment scheme. Introduces the term **"random binary choice (RBC)"** as an umbrella for the entire class of mechanisms where subjects answer binary questions and one is randomly selected for payment. Shows that BDM (value), belief elicitation (Karni 2009), Holt-Laury (risk), cardinal utility elicitation, and even full preference ranking are all instances of the same RBC class. All are IC under monotonicity alone (Azrieli, Chambers & Healy 2018, JPE).

### Key Findings

- **RBC as umbrella concept:** "There is an entire class of elicitation mechanisms — which I call *random binary choice (RBC)* mechanisms — that are procedurally identical to the BDM mechanism and can be explained in the same way" (p. 1). The class includes: value elicitation (BDM), cardinal utility elicitation, belief elicitation (Karni 2009), risk aversion elicitation (Holt-Laury), and full preference ranking.
- **All RBC mechanisms are IC under monotonicity** (Azrieli et al. 2018, JPE). They do NOT require expected utility. Caveat: if choice objects are lotteries, monotonicity + reduction of compound lotteries implies EU. So if you distrust EU but trust reduction, you should also distrust RBC mechanisms.
- **Belief elicitation via RBC (Section IV):** Present subjects with 100 binary questions: "Would you rather have: $20 if E occurs, or X% chance of $20?" The switch point reveals their belief. This is the MPL for beliefs, = the Karni (2009) mechanism, = the "hidden MPL" from Healy & Leo (2025).
- **Holt & Smith (2016) finding cited:** In belief elicitation, the list presentation and the single-response BDM produce similar accuracy, and both outperform the BQSR.
- **Brown & Healy (2018, EER) finding:** Subjects do NOT report truthfully when the RBC is presented as one list on a single screen, but DO report truthfully when each row is shown on a separate screen in random order. "The list causes subjects to view all the choices as one large choice rather than separate choices, and this causes them to report a different answer." However, Healy notes that asking each row separately is usually impractical, and given Holt & Smith (2016) results, the list presentation works well enough.
- **The note explicitly states belief elicitation via RBC was "first done by Grether (1981)"** and independently discovered by Holt (1986), Mobius et al. (2013), and Karni (2009). "But it really just comes straight out of Savage (1954)."

### Method
Pedagogical note / literature synthesis. No original data.

### Relevance to Our Project
- **Origin of the "RBC" terminology** that appears in some of our literature references. Healy dropped this term in the published 2025 chapter with Leo, replacing it with "MPL" and "single-response BDM." We should cite this note when explaining the terminological history.
- **The Brown & Healy (2018) finding is directly relevant to our design.** If subjects view the MPL as "one big choice" rather than independent binary decisions, the UJS property (each row independently justifiable) may not operate as theory predicts. This could explain why MPL still shows some BIC failures even though each row should have a uniquely justifiable action.
- **Azrieli et al. (2018, JPE)** establishes IC for the Random Problem Selection (RPS) mechanism — paying one randomly chosen task in a multi-task experiment — under monotonicity. The belief BDM is an instance of RPS (their footnote 16), so the general result applies. Healy & Leo (2025) derive belief BDM IC as "a simple application of Azrieli et al. (2018)" (their Proposition 4). But note: Azrieli et al. is about the general RPS framework, not specifically about belief elicitation. The belief BDM mechanism itself was first implemented experimentally by Ducharme & Donnell (1973, "bets mode" — in psychology) and Grether (1981, in economics), and was independently rediscovered by Karni (2009). Note: Savage (1971) developed the *proper scoring rule* method, which is a different mechanism family — do not conflate with BDM-for-beliefs.
- **Historical priority:** Ducharme & Donnell (1973) is the earliest known experimental use of the SBDM for beliefs (Burfurd & Wilkening 2018, fn. 2). Grether (1981) is the earliest in economics. Karni (2009) is an independent rediscovery. See also Holt (1986), Mobius et al. (2013).

### CS Comments
- This is a must-cite note for our paper's terminology section. It explains why different papers use different names for the same mechanism class, and it establishes the theoretical unity of BDM, MPL, Karni, and Holt-Laury under the RBC/monotonicity umbrella.
- The Brown & Healy (2018) finding about list-as-one-choice vs. separate-screens is a potential confound for our MPL treatment. If subjects view the MPL holistically, they may not be making independent row-by-row decisions, which undermines the UJS argument for MPL superiority. We should consider whether to present MPL rows separately or as a full list.
- Azrieli, Chambers & Healy (2018, JPE) needs to be added to our reading notes — it's the general RPS IC result under monotonicity. For our project, we cite it for the general framework, but cite Karni (2009) for the specific belief BDM mechanism IC.

### Open Questions
- Does the Brown & Healy (2018) list-as-one-choice effect operate in belief elicitation specifically, or only in the value/risk contexts they tested?
- Should we present our MPL treatment as a full list or as separate binary questions? The former is standard practice; the latter is more theoretically defensible but impractical.

## 17. Holt & Smith (2016) — Synchronized Lottery Choice Menu for Belief Elicitation

**Citation:** Holt, C.A. & Smith, A.M. (2016). Belief Elicitation with a Synchronized Lottery Choice Menu That Is Invariant to Risk Attitudes. *American Economic Journal: Microeconomics*, 8(1), 110-139.
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Holt_Smith_2016_AEJMicro.pdf`

### Summary
Proposes a two-stage "lottery choice" (LC) menu for eliciting subjective probabilities that is IC regardless of risk attitudes (binary payoffs make utility cancel out). The LC is a discretized MPL version of the probabilistic BDM: Stage 1 is a coarse 11-row grid (0%, 10%, ..., 100%), Stage 2 zooms into 1% increments around the crossover point. Compares LC to single-response BDM and two versions of QSR in a Bayesian updating task (N=144, UVA undergrads). Healy & Leo (2025) credit this as the first paper to explicitly represent the probabilistic BDM as an MPL.

### Key Findings

- **Average absolute deviation from Bayes' predictions:** LC = 7.0, QSR-chances = 8.6, BDM = 9.9, QSR-numbers = 11.8
- **LC vs. BDM:** p = 0.10 (two-tailed randomization test) — close but not significant at 10%
- **LC vs. QSR-numbers:** p = 0.008 (significant at 1%)
- **Proportion of exact Bayesian responses (within ±1):** LC = 35.7%, BDM = 20.0%, QSR-chances = 16.8%, QSR-numbers = 11.1%. LC vs. BDM: p = 0.021.
- **Boundary decisions (0 or 100):** LC = 3%, BDM = 10%, QSR-chances = 15%, QSR-numbers = 9%. LC vs. BDM: p = 0.046.
- **Subjects with at least one boundary decision:** LC = 14%, BDM = 39%, QSR-chances = 61%, QSR-numbers = 33%.
- **QSR pull-to-extremes (not center):** When Bayes' posterior differs from 50%, QSR treatments pull toward extremes — 55-57% of deviations toward 0/100 vs. 26% toward center. LC and BDM show balanced deviations. This contradicts the standard "pull-to-center" narrative for QSR under risk aversion.
- **Decision time:** LC = 31s (significantly higher, p < 0.0001), BDM = 15s, QSR-chances = 17s, QSR-numbers = 20s. LC is the slowest.

### Method
Between-subjects, 4 treatments: LC (lottery choice menu), BDM (probability pricing), QSR-chances (labeled with probabilities), QSR-numbers (neutral labels). N=144 (36 per treatment), UVA undergrads, in-lab. Bayesian updating task: two cups (Red: 2 red + 1 blue marble; Blue: 2 blue + 1 red), 0-4 draws with replacement, 12 rounds. Payment: $6 show-up + earnings from ALL rounds ($2 or $0 per round). Average total ~$21. Common random seed across sessions.

**The "synchronized" feature:** In each LC row, both the event lottery and the random lottery pay the same two amounts ($2 or $0). Because payoffs are binary, utility cancels out and the choice depends only on which lottery has a higher winning probability. This is why the mechanism is invariant to risk attitudes.

**BDM implementation:** Subject enters "chances out of 100." Random number drawn; if random < belief, subject gets event lottery; if random > belief, subject gets random lottery with that probability.

**LC implementation:** Stage 1: 11-row coarse grid, single crossover enforced. Stage 2: fine grid (1% increments) around crossover. One row randomly selected for payment.

### Relevance to Our Project
- **BDM-MPL equivalence established:** The LC is literally a discretized BDM presented as an MPL. The continuous LC "also describes a BDM procedure where R is a number analogous to a selling price" (p. 125). This confirms Healy & Leo's attribution and our taxonomy's formal equivalence claim.
- **Format matters despite theoretical equivalence:** LC outperforms BDM on exact Bayesian responses (35.7% vs. 20.0%, p = 0.021) and boundary decisions (3% vs. 10%, p = 0.046), even though both are theoretically identical mechanisms. The difference must come from format/comprehension, not incentive structure.
- **Comprehension channel:** Authors argue the LC "choice menu structure is more transparent and intuitive than the Becker-DeGroot-Marschak procedure" (p. 110). Row-by-row binary comparisons make the implicit contingent reasoning in BDM explicit. This directly supports our Hypothesis A (comprehension failure).
- **Risk invariance shared:** Both BDM and LC use binary payoffs, so risk attitudes cannot explain the accuracy difference. The advantage is purely comprehension/format.
- **LC as benchmark for our design:** If we include an MPL treatment arm, the Holt-Smith LC is the most well-documented implementation. Their two-stage format (coarse then fine) achieves 1% precision with only ~20 choices.
- **Decision time tradeoff:** LC takes twice as long as BDM (31s vs. 15s). In online settings, this could increase attrition or reduce attention. But subjects also think more carefully.
- **QSR pull-to-extremes is surprising** and relevant if we compare BDM to scoring rules. The standard narrative (risk aversion pulls QSR toward center) may not hold when all rounds are paid.

### Open Questions
- The LC advantage over BDM is p = 0.10 on average absolute deviation (not significant) but p = 0.021 on exact Bayesian responses. How should we interpret this — does the LC reduce large errors or increase precision?
- Single-crossover enforcement inflates consistency. How much of the LC advantage comes from this constraint vs. the row-by-row format? A BDM variant with consistency flagging could test this.
- Would the LC advantage persist online with less attentive subjects?
- The pay-all-rounds design means every round matters — this could interact with mechanism effects differently than the standard random-round-selection design.

## 18. Azrieli, Chambers & Healy (2018) — Incentives in Experiments: A Theoretical Analysis

**Citation:** Azrieli, Y., Chambers, C.P. & Healy, P.J. (2018). Incentives in Experiments: A Theoretical Analysis. *Journal of Political Economy*, 126(4), 1472-1503.
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Azrieli_Chamber_Healy_2018_JPE.pdf`

### Summary
Develops a general theoretical framework for analyzing payment mechanisms in experiments with multiple tasks. The paper's two main results are: (1) under statewise monotonicity and nothing else, the Random Problem Selection (RPS) mechanism -- paying for one randomly chosen task -- is essentially the only incentive compatible mechanism; (2) under a "no complementarities at the top" (NCaT) condition and nothing else, the pay-all mechanism is essentially the only IC mechanism. Importantly, the framework places no structure on the choice objects X and does not require expected utility, objective probabilities, or any specific decision-theoretic model.

### Key Findings

- **Proposition 0 (IC is never free):** If every extension of preferences to P(X) is admissible, then there exists an IC payment mechanism if and only if k = 1 (the experiment has only one decision problem). In other words, in multi-task experiments the researcher MUST make some assumptions about preferences over P(X) to achieve IC.

- **Definition 2 (Statewise monotonicity):** The extension >=* is a monotonic extension of >= if: (i) f dominates g (written f ⊒ g, meaning f(omega) >= g(omega) for all states omega in Omega with f(omega), g(omega) in X) implies f >=* g, and (ii) f strictly dominates g (f ⊒ g and f(omega) > g(omega) for some omega) implies f >* g. Monotonicity places no restriction on how nonsingleton bundles are evaluated. Wealth effects, portfolio effects, and hedging are all possible under monotonicity. It restricts only mechanisms that select (nonbundle) elements from X.

- **Definition 3 (RPS mechanism):** A payment mechanism phi is an RPS mechanism if there is a fixed partition {Omega_1, ..., Omega_k} of Omega with each Omega_i nonempty such that, for each i in {1,...,k} and m in M: omega in Omega_i implies phi(m)(omega) = m_i. In other words, if event Omega_i obtains, the subject is paid what she chose in decision problem i. The only difference between various RPS mechanisms is the randomizing device used.

- **Proposition 1 (RPS is IC under monotonicity):** If all admissible extensions satisfy monotonicity, then the RPS payment mechanism is incentive compatible. The proof is simple: deviating from truth-telling in some D_i results in an act that gives a less preferred outcome in event Omega_i and has no effect in any other event. Monotonicity guarantees such a deviation is never preferred. Crucially, this does NOT require an objective randomizing device -- it holds even if subjects perceive the RPS randomization as ambiguous.

- **Theorem 1 (Characterization under monotonicity):** Suppose preferences on X are strict and all extensions satisfying monotonicity are admissible. A mechanism phi is IC if and only if it is a random set selection (RSS) mechanism in which: (1) D_i is in SI(P^phi) for each i (every decision problem is "surely identified" from the mechanism's payment sets), and (2) phi(M_R) intersection phi(M_NR) = empty set (rationalizable and nonrationalizable messages pay different things). In most applications, the set of decision problems is not rich enough for non-RPS mechanisms to exist, so the RPS mechanism is the UNIQUE IC mechanism.

- **Footnote 16 (BDM as RPS):** "The well-known Becker et al. (1964; BDM) mechanism for eliciting the subjective value of an object (and its counterpart for eliciting probabilities, as in Grether [1981] and Karni [2009]) is an example with such a restriction. There, X = {(0,0), (1,1), (1,2), ..., (1,N)}, where (1,n) means 'receiving the object and paying $n' and (0,0) means 'not receiving the object and paying $0.' Preferences on X in this environment are assumed to satisfy single crossing, so that if (1,n) > (0,0), then for all n' < n, (1,n') > (0,0). Given >, the value of the object is n* = max{n : (1,n) > (0,0)}. Under single crossing, announcing n* reveals the entire preference relation over X. Thus, the BDM is simply an RPS mechanism in which D_n = {(0,0), (1,n)} for each n in {1,...,N} and an announcement of n* is interpreted as m_n = (1,n) if n <= n* and m_n = (0,0) if n > n*."

- **Definition 9 (No Complementarities at the Top, NCaT):** The extension >=* is an NCaT extension of >= if, for every (x_1, ..., x_k) in mu(>=) and every (y_1, ..., y_k) in the Cartesian product of D_i's: the bundle of favorites is weakly preferred to any other bundle, i.e., sum_i x_i >=* sum_i y_i, with strict preference if there is at least one i for which y_i is not in mu_i(>=). NCaT guarantees that a bundle of favorite elements is preferred to every other bundle the subject could receive. It allows complementarities among lower-ranked alternatives and smaller-sized bundles; it only restricts complementarities "at the top."

- **Proposition 2 (Pay-all is IC under NCaT):** The pay-all mechanism is incentive compatible if every admissible extension satisfies NCaT.

- **Theorem 2 (Characterization under NCaT):** Suppose preferences are strict and all extensions satisfying NCaT are admissible. Let phi^PA be the pay-all mechanism. A mechanism phi is IC if and only if: (1) phi(m)(omega) = phi^PA(m)(omega) for all m in M_R and omega in Omega, (2) phi(M) is a subset of phi^PA(M), and (3) phi(M_R) intersection phi(M_NR) = empty set. In most experiments, the only IC mechanism is the pay-all mechanism.

- **Fact 1 (Monotonicity + reduction implies independence):** Assume X is convex. If >=* satisfies monotonicity and reduction (of compound lotteries), then >= satisfies independence (the vNM independence axiom). This is the key result reconciling the apparent paradox between the paper's Proposition 1 (monotonicity alone suffices for RPS IC) and the older literature's claim that EU is needed: Holt (1986) and Karni & Safra (1987) assume reduction, under which any violation of EU also violates monotonicity.

- **Fact 1.1:** If X is a convex set of objective lotteries, >= violates the independence axiom, and >=* satisfies reduction (of compound lotteries), then monotonicity of >=* is violated. Thus there exist experiments for which the RPS mechanism is not incentive compatible for non-EU subjects who satisfy reduction.

- **Fact 1.2:** If X is a convex space of acts mapping a finite state space Theta into a convex set of outcomes Y, >= is not ambiguity neutral, and >=* satisfies reduction ("order reversal"), then monotonicity of >=* is violated. Parallel result for ambiguity: ambiguity-averse subjects who satisfy order reversal will violate monotonicity, enabling hedging across RPS tasks.

- **Feedback and experimentation:** The framework is static (all uncertainty resolves after all choices are announced). If feedback between decisions can depend on choices, incentive compatibility cannot be assured. But if feedback is completely independent of the subject's actions (e.g., via strategy method), then the RPS mechanism is IC under an appropriately redefined monotonicity (shown in online appendix C).

### Method
Pure theory with a decision-theoretic/mechanism design framework. No structure assumed on the choice objects X. Preferences >= over X are complete and transitive but need not conform to any decision-theoretic model (no expected utility, no subjective probabilities, no Savage axioms required). The key innovation is separating preferences over X from their extension >=* to P(X) = B(X)^Omega (the space of acts over bundles). IC is defined as: for every admissible extension, truth-telling weakly dominates, with strict dominance for non-truthful messages. The paper also includes an empirical survey of payment mechanism usage in 59 experimental papers published in 2011 across top-5 journals and Experimental Economics.

### Relevance to Our Project

- **The general result that grounds belief BDM IC.** The belief BDM (Karni 2009) is an instance of RPS as shown in footnote 16. Healy & Leo (2025) derive belief BDM IC as "a simple application of Azrieli et al. (2018)" (their Proposition 4). This paper is the theoretical foundation we cite for WHY the mechanism is IC -- Karni (2009) provides the specific belief mechanism, but Azrieli et al. provides the general result that RPS is IC under monotonicity.

- **What monotonicity means for belief elicitation:** In the belief BDM context, monotonicity says: if act f pays a weakly better outcome than act g in every state (i.e., for every possible realization of the random comparison probability), the subject weakly prefers f to g. Since the belief BDM can be decomposed into binary decisions (each asking "do you prefer the event bet or a lottery with probability r?"), monotonicity says: getting your preferred option in any one binary decision while holding the rest fixed is weakly preferred. This is the same as the "statewise monotonicity" in the Healy & Leo (2025) IC hierarchy (their Axiom 5).

- **The IC hierarchy confirmed:** This paper establishes the theoretical ordering: monotonicity (sufficient for RPS/BDM IC) < reduction + monotonicity = independence/EU < NCaT (sufficient for pay-all IC). The belief BDM sits at the bottom of this hierarchy -- requiring only the weakest assumption. This makes BIC failure more puzzling: the mechanism with the weakest theoretical demands fails behaviorally.

- **Monotonicity violations and when RPS fails:** The paper identifies several sources of monotonicity violation: (1) non-EU preferences combined with reduction of compound lotteries (Fact 1.1); (2) ambiguity aversion combined with order reversal (Fact 1.2); (3) ex ante fairness concerns (Diamond 1967 -- a dictator may prefer the randomization itself); (4) "irrational diversification" or probability matching (Loomes 1998, Rubinstein 2002). For our project, the key insight is that monotonicity violations are about how subjects evaluate the COMPOUND lottery created by RPS, not about the individual tasks. If subjects don't "reduce" compound lotteries, their behavior in the BDM may be non-monotonic even if their underlying preferences are well-behaved.

- **The reduction paradox for our project:** Without reduction (or a similar axiom), properties of >= over X are "completely unrelated to the incentive compatibility of the RPS mechanism" (p. 1489). This is crucial: our subjects may have perfectly good beliefs but fail to navigate the compound lottery structure of the BDM. The mechanism's IC depends on how subjects process the randomization layer, not on whether they have well-defined beliefs. This supports framing BIC failure as a cognitive/comprehension issue rather than a preference issue.

- **The empirical evidence section (Sec. V) provides important context:** Only 23% of papers in their 2011 survey explicitly justify their chosen payment mechanism. Tests of monotonicity show mixed results -- Brown & Healy (2018) find monotonicity violation when all decisions are on one screen (p = .040) but NOT when each decision is on a separate screen in random order (p = .697). This connects to the presentation/format effects documented in our other reading notes.

### CS Comments


### Open Questions
- The paper treats IC as an all-or-nothing property at the individual level (either the mechanism is IC for all admissible preferences, or it is not). But BIC is about population-level behavior. How should we connect the individual-level theory (monotonicity implies IC for each person) to the population-level observation (many people fail to report truthfully)? Is it that many people violate monotonicity? Or that they satisfy monotonicity over X but cannot cognitively implement the optimal strategy?
- Footnote 16 describes BDM as RPS with the single-crossing restriction on X. For belief BDM specifically, what is the analogous restriction? Presumably it is that the subject prefers the event bet over a p-chance lottery if and only if she believes the event has probability > p. This is the "single crossing in beliefs" that replaces single crossing in values.
- The paper shows that if subjects don't satisfy reduction, the RPS mechanism's IC is not about individual-task preferences at all -- it's about compound lottery evaluation. Could we test this directly by measuring whether subjects satisfy reduction (Dustan, Koutout & Leo 2023 find only 30% do) and correlating reduction compliance with BIC compliance?
- Brown & Healy (2018) find monotonicity is violated on one screen but not on separate screens. Our BDM presents one task at a time (single-response format). Does this mean we should expect monotonicity to hold for our subjects? If so, the BIC failure must come from somewhere other than monotonicity violation.

## 19. Karni (2009) — A Mechanism for Eliciting Probabilities

**Citation:** Karni, E. (2009). A Mechanism for Eliciting Probabilities. *Econometrica*, 77(2), 603-606.
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Karni_2009.pdf`

### Summary
Introduces a direct revelation mechanism for eliciting subjective probabilities that achieves dominant-strategy incentive compatibility under probabilistic sophistication and dominance, without requiring expected utility or risk neutrality. The mechanism compares the agent's reported probability to a uniformly drawn random number: if the report exceeds the draw, the agent receives a bet on the event; otherwise, the agent receives a lottery with the drawn probability. Truthful reporting is the unique dominant strategy. The paper also shows that this mechanism avoids the incentive-accuracy tradeoff inherent in proper scoring rules and de Finetti's promissory notes method, where increasing stakes to motivate effort simultaneously biases the probability report through marginal utility effects.

### Key Findings / Theoretical Results

**Setup and definitions:**

- *S* = set of states; one state is the true state. Subsets of *S* are events. An event *obtains* if the true state belongs to it.
- *Simple acts* = mappings from *S* to real numbers (monetary payoffs) with finite images.
- A *bet on event E* is a simple act paying *x* dollars if *E* obtains and *y* dollars otherwise, with x > y, denoted x_E y.
- A *simple lottery* l(p, x, y) = [x, p; y, (1-p)] is a finite list of monetary prizes with corresponding probabilities.
- *D* = union of the sets of simple acts and lotteries.
- Preference relation >= on *D*, with strict part > and indifference ~.

**Probabilistic sophistication (Machina & Schmeidler 1995):**
A preference relation >= on *D* restricted to finite acts is probabilistically sophisticated if it ranks acts or lotteries solely on the basis of their implied probability distributions over outcomes. In particular, if pi is the probability measure implicit in >=, then for all acts *f* and lotteries l(p, x, y) with p in [0,1]: pi(f^{-1}(x)) = p implies x_{f^{-1}(x)} y ~ l(p, x, y). That is, an act and a lottery with the same probability distribution over outcomes are indifferent.

**Dominance assumption:**
The agent's preference relation >= on *D* displays dominance in the sense that l(p, x, y) >= l(p', x, y) for all x > y if and only if p >= p'. Higher probability of the better prize is weakly preferred.

**The IC theorem (informal but complete -- no formal theorem statement in the paper):**
Under probabilistic sophistication and dominance, truthful reporting (mu = pi(E)) is the agent's **unique dominant strategy** in the elicitation mechanism.

**Proof (verbatim reconstruction of the argument):**
Suppose the agent reports mu > pi(E). Consider three cases for the random draw r:
1. If r <= pi(E): agent's payoff is beta = x_E y regardless of reporting mu or pi(E). No difference.
2. If r >= mu: agent's payoff is l(r, x, y) regardless of reporting mu or pi(E). No difference.
3. If r in (pi(E), mu): agent's payoff is beta (the event bet). Had he reported pi(E) instead, his payoff would have been l(r, x, y). But r > pi(E), which by probabilistic sophistication and dominance implies l(r, x, y) > beta. The agent is **strictly worse off** reporting mu instead of pi(E).

A symmetric argument applies when mu < pi(E): if r in (mu, pi(E)), the agent gets l(r, x, y) but would have gotten beta had he reported pi(E). Since r < pi(E), probabilistic sophistication and dominance imply beta > l(r, x, y). Again, the agent is strictly worse off.

Since for any mu != pi(E) there exists a positive-probability interval of r values where the agent is strictly worse off, and no r value where the agent is strictly better off, truthful reporting is the unique dominant strategy.

**Key insight of the proof:** The mechanism creates a region of r values where misreporting "flips" the outcome from the preferred option to the less-preferred option. Probabilistic sophistication is what allows the comparison between the bet beta and the lottery l(r, x, y): the bet x_E y is equivalent to the lottery l(pi(E), x, y), and dominance then ranks l(r, x, y) vs. l(pi(E), x, y) by comparing r to pi(E).

**Extensions noted in the paper:**
- Many agents: run the mechanism separately for each agent. Alternatively, the random number r can be generated endogenously using the highest reported value as r and treating all other reports as mu (see Karni 2008).
- Finitely many events: run the mechanism separately for each event.
- Effort incentives: if the payoff difference (x - y) is sufficiently large, the agent is induced to exert the effort necessary to arrive at an accurate assessment.

**Equivalent auction mechanism:**
The mechanism has an equivalent formulation as a continuous ascending bid auction between the agent and a "dummy bidder." Starting at 0, the bid increases continuously. The dummy bidder stays in as long as the bid < r and drops out when the bid = r. The agent is awarded l(r, x, y) if he is the first to quit, and beta otherwise. The agent's dominant strategy is to stay in as long as the bid < pi(E) and quit at pi(E). This is the "clock" version of the belief BDM, analogous to the ascending clock for value BDM.

**Comparison with proper scoring rules (Section 3):**
The proper scoring rule (quadratic) pays the agent a score -r(delta_E - mu)^2, where r is a positive constant, mu is the reported probability of E, and delta_E is the indicator function of E. Let w-tilde be the agent's random wealth with CDF F, distributed independently of E. The agent maximizes:

Max_mu [ pi(E) * integral u(w - r(1 - mu)^2) dF(w|E) + (1 - pi(E)) * integral u(w - r*mu^2) dF(w|E^c) ]

The necessary condition yields:

mu*(r) / (1 - mu*(r)) = K(r) * pi(E) / (1 - pi(E))

where K(r) = [integral u'(w - r(1-mu*(r))^2) dF(w|E)] / [integral u'(w - r*mu*(r)^2) dF(w|E^c)]

So mu*(r) = pi(E) if and only if K(r) = 1. Unless the agent is risk neutral, K(r) != 1, and the scoring rule method confounds subjective probabilities with marginal utilities. Increasing r (to motivate effort) increases the bias:
- Risk averse + pi(E) != 1/2: mu*(r) is biased toward 1/2, bias increases with r.
- Risk loving + pi(E) < 1/2: mu*(r) biased toward 0; pi(E) > 1/2: biased toward 1; bias increases with r.

This is the incentive-accuracy tradeoff: large r motivates effort but biases the report; small r avoids bias but provides no incentive for effort. Karni's mechanism avoids this tradeoff entirely because IC holds regardless of risk attitudes and the incentive level (x - y) can be set independently.

**No-stakes requirement (final paragraph):**
The accuracy of the mechanism depends **critically** on the agent having no stake in the event of interest. If the agent has a stake in E, the evaluations of the payoffs of the bet and the lotteries become event-dependent, and the preference relation does not exhibit probabilistic sophistication. This limitation applies equally to all other elicitation methods (Kadane & Winkler 1988). Jaffray & Karni (1999) and Karni (1999) developed methods to overcome this difficulty.

### The Mechanism (step by step)

1. Fix an event E whose probability is to be elicited, and two monetary prizes x > y.
2. Define the event bet: beta := x_E y (pays x if E obtains, y otherwise).
3. Draw a random number r uniformly from [0, 1].
4. The agent submits a report mu in [0, 1] of his subjective probability of E.
5. **Payoff rule:**
   - If mu >= r: the agent receives beta (the event bet x_E y).
   - If mu < r: the agent receives the lottery l(r, x, y) = [x, r; y, (1-r)].
6. The agent's payoff is then determined by the realization of the event (if he got beta) or the realization of the lottery (if he got l(r, x, y)).

**Note on the payoff structure:** The agent is always comparing two objects that deliver the same two prizes x and y. The bet delivers x with probability pi(E) (from the agent's perspective). The lottery delivers x with probability r (objectively). Under probabilistic sophistication, the agent prefers whichever has the higher probability of x. So the agent wants beta when pi(E) > r and wants l(r, x, y) when r > pi(E). Reporting mu = pi(E) ensures the agent gets the preferred option for every possible r.

### IC Proof Sketch

The proof works by showing that any deviation from truthful reporting creates a "window of regret" -- a positive-probability interval of r values where the agent receives the less-preferred option instead of the preferred one -- while never creating any interval where the agent benefits. The key steps are:

1. **Probabilistic sophistication** allows the comparison: beta ~ l(pi(E), x, y), so the agent's preference between beta and l(r, x, y) reduces to comparing pi(E) vs. r.
2. **Dominance** provides the ranking: l(r, x, y) > l(pi(E), x, y) ~ beta when r > pi(E), and beta ~ l(pi(E), x, y) > l(r, x, y) when pi(E) > r.
3. **The mechanism structure** ensures that reporting mu = pi(E) makes the agent receive beta exactly when r <= pi(E) (i.e., when beta is preferred) and l(r, x, y) exactly when r > pi(E) (i.e., when the lottery is preferred). Any other report mu != pi(E) introduces a range of r values where this matching breaks down.

### Assumptions Required

1. **Probabilistic sophistication** (Machina & Schmeidler 1995): The agent ranks acts and lotteries solely by their implied probability distributions over outcomes. This is weaker than expected utility -- it does not require linearity in probabilities. It DOES require that the agent has well-defined subjective probabilities and can compare an act to a lottery with the same probability distribution. Rank-dependent utility satisfies probabilistic sophistication. **Caveat:** Standard prospect theory with probability weighting may NOT satisfy probabilistic sophistication if subjective probabilities are weighted differently than objective ones -- the condition requires that an act and a lottery with the same probability distribution are ranked identically, which probability weighting over subjective events can violate. This matters for our project: if subjects use prospect-theory-like probability weighting, the IC result may not hold even theoretically.

2. **Dominance** (monotonicity in probability): Among lotteries over the same two prizes x > y, the agent prefers the one with higher probability of the better prize. Formally: l(p, x, y) >= l(p', x, y) for all x > y iff p >= p'. This is extremely weak -- it is satisfied by virtually all decision theories including prospect theory, rank-dependent utility, and disappointment aversion.

3. **No stakes in the event:** The agent must not have any prior stake in whether event E obtains or not. If the agent's wealth depends on E, the evaluation of the bet and lottery become state-dependent, violating probabilistic sophistication. This is the "no-stake requirement" discussed in the final paragraph.

4. **Implicitly: the agent understands the mechanism.** The proof assumes the agent can correctly evaluate the bet beta as equivalent to l(pi(E), x, y) and can compare this to l(r, x, y) for all possible r. This is never stated as a formal assumption in the paper but is assumed throughout.

**What is NOT required:**
- Expected utility (EU)
- Risk neutrality
- Objective probabilities over S
- Reduction of compound lotteries
- Independence axiom (vNM)
- Any specific functional form for preferences

### Relevance to Our Project

- **This is the theoretical foundation for our entire project.** Karni (2009) establishes that the probabilistic BDM (matching probabilities) is incentive compatible for eliciting subjective probabilities under assumptions far weaker than EU. Our project asks: given that the mechanism is theoretically IC, why does it fail behaviorally (BIC)?

- **The mechanism requires implicit assumption #4 -- comprehension -- which Karni never discusses.** The entire proof assumes the agent can evaluate beta = x_E y as equivalent to l(pi(E), x, y) and can perform the comparison with l(r, x, y). This is a non-trivial cognitive operation: the agent must (a) hold a belief pi(E), (b) understand that the bet beta is "like" a lottery with that probability, (c) understand that reporting mu determines a threshold for which option she receives, and (d) recognize that the optimal threshold is exactly pi(E). Steps (b)-(d) are the comprehension bottleneck that our project studies.

- **The connection to BDM for values is structural, not just analogical.** Just as BDM for values compares a good to a price (with a random price determining the trade), Karni's mechanism compares a bet to a lottery (with a random probability determining which is received). In both cases, the agent sets a "cutoff" (WTP or probability), and IC requires the agent to set the cutoff at the true value/probability. The cognitive challenge is the same: understanding that the mechanism's structure makes truthful cutoff-setting a dominant strategy.

- **The proper scoring rule comparison in Section 3 is directly relevant.** Karni shows formally that QSR confounds beliefs with risk attitudes (K(r) != 1 for non-risk-neutral agents), with bias increasing in the incentive parameter r. This is the incentive-accuracy tradeoff. The BDM avoids this tradeoff theoretically -- but if subjects don't understand the mechanism, the BDM's theoretical advantage is moot. Our project tests whether the BDM's theoretical IC advantage translates to behavioral IC advantage over simpler but theoretically weaker mechanisms (BSR, flat fee).

- **The no-stakes requirement is satisfied in our experimental design.** Our urn scenarios involve events (ball colors) in which subjects have no prior stake. This assumption is not a concern for our project.

- **The "equivalent auction mechanism" (ascending clock) maps directly to Hao & Houser (2012) and Brown et al. (2026).** Karni notes that the mechanism can be implemented as an ascending clock where the agent drops out at pi(E). This is the clock mechanism that Hao & Houser test in their belief elicitation experiment, finding it outperforms the single-response (declarative) BDM. The clock censors naive misreports but does not improve understanding -- it bypasses the comprehension problem rather than solving it.

- **The paper is remarkably silent on behavioral implementation.** In 4 pages, there is no discussion of whether subjects can actually navigate the mechanism, whether the comparison between a bet and a lottery is cognitively natural, or what happens if subjects don't satisfy probabilistic sophistication. The entire treatment is normative/mechanism-design. This silence is the gap our project fills.

### Open Questions
- Karni assumes probabilistic sophistication, which requires well-defined subjective probabilities. But Azrieli et al. (2018) show that the same IC result follows from the weaker assumption of statewise monotonicity alone, without requiring the agent to have well-defined probabilities or to be probabilistically sophisticated. How should we position our project relative to these two results? Karni provides the specific mechanism; Azrieli et al. provide the general result. The answer may be: cite Karni for the mechanism definition and Azrieli et al. for the IC result under the weakest assumptions.
- The proof relies on the agent comparing beta to l(r, x, y) for all possible r. In practice, subjects see a single realization of r after submitting mu. Do subjects reason ex ante (over all possible r, as the proof requires) or ex post (conditional on a specific r, which may lead to regret-based reasoning)? If subjects reason ex post, the "window of regret" argument may not be psychologically compelling.
- Karni notes that effort incentives increase with (x - y). But larger (x - y) also increases the stakes of misreporting and may increase anxiety/confusion. Is there an optimal (x - y) that balances effort incentives against cognitive load? This is an empirical question our design could address.
- The equivalent auction mechanism (ascending clock) is presented as a theoretical equivalence. But Hao & Houser (2012) show the two implementations produce different behavior. This means the "equivalence" holds only for rational agents -- for boundedly rational agents, the interface matters. Can we characterize exactly where the equivalence breaks down?
- [Note from broader literature, not from Karni's paper itself:] The mechanism was independently discovered by Ducharme & Donnell (1973, "bets mode"), Grether (1981), and Holt & Smith (2009/2016) — Karni (2009) does not cite these predecessors. (Note: Savage 1971 developed the proper scoring rule method, a different mechanism family — not the BDM-for-beliefs.) Why has this mechanism been independently rediscovered so many times? Is it because the BDM-for-values idea naturally suggests a probability analog, or is there a deeper structural reason?

## 20. Brown & Healy (2018) — Separated Decisions

**Citation:** Brown, A.L. & Healy, P.J. (2018). Separated Decisions. *European Economic Review*, 101, 20--34.
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Brown_Healy_2018-MonotonicityVsFraming.pdf`

### Summary
Tests whether the random problem selection (RPS) mechanism is incentive compatible using a 20-row multiple price list over lotteries. Finds that RPS fails incentive compatibility when all 20 decisions are displayed together as a list on one screen, but IC is restored when each decision is shown on a separate screen in random order ("separated decisions"). The failure in the list format is driven by a violation of monotonicity -- the key assumption for RPS to be IC per Azrieli et al. (2018; WP 2016) -- and is confounded with a list framing effect that happens to nearly perfectly offset the monotonicity violation at the aggregate level.

### Key Findings

**Experiment 1 (List format, N=181 at Ohio State):**
- Three treatments: L-RPS (list, RPS payment), L-14 (list, pay row 14 only = Framed Control), O-14 (one choice only, row 14 = One Choice)
- % choosing risky lottery in row 14: L-RPS 51.7%, L-14 70.0%, O-14 55.7% (n=60 per treatment)
- Chi-squared test across all three: p=0.0999
- **Monotonicity test (L-RPS vs. L-14):** p=0.041. Under monotonicity, these should be identical since L-14 pays only row 14. The significant difference rejects monotonicity and thus IC of the RPS mechanism in the list format.
- **Framing test (O-14 vs. L-14):** p=0.051. Marginally insignificant, suggesting a possible list framing effect.
- Logistic regressions (Table 4) confirm: monotonicity violation coefficient = -0.781 to -0.883 (p=0.026 to 0.041); list framing effect coefficient = -0.617 to -0.716 (p=0.071 to 0.106).
- Key insight: if only L-RPS vs O-14 had been compared (as in prior literature), p=0.485 because monotonicity violation and list framing effect nearly cancel out.

**Experiment 2 (Separated format, N=124 at Ohio State):**
- Two treatments: S-RPS (separated, RPS payment), S-14 (separated, pay row 14 only)
- Each row shown on a separate screen, order randomized independently for each subject
- % choosing risky in row 14: S-RPS 59.0%, S-14 55.6%, O-14 55.7% (O-14 from Exp 1)
- Chi-squared test: p=0.909 across all three treatments
- **Monotonicity test (S-RPS vs. S-14):** p=0.697. Cannot reject monotonicity. IC is restored.
- Fisher's exact test for monotonicity: p=0.857
- Logistic regressions (Table 7): no treatment effects significant, with or without controls
- **Switch-back behavior:** Significantly more multiple switching in S-RPS (32.8%) than L-RPS (5.0%), chi-squared p=0.00013. But these are genuine preference intransitivities (since IC holds), not artifacts.

**Literature comparison (Table 9):**
- Every prior test using LIST format that includes a Framed Control rejects IC: Starmer & Sugden (1991) C vs D p=0.043; Brown & Healy L-RPS vs L-14 p=0.041
- Every test using SEPARATED format fails to reject IC: Cubitt et al. (1998) treatments 3.1 vs 3.3 p=0.685; Cubitt et al. 3.2 vs 3.3 p=0.120; Cox et al. (2014b) all comparisons p>0.12; Brown & Healy S-RPS vs S-14 p=0.697
- Pattern is perfectly clean: list format -> IC violated; separated format -> IC holds

**Row 20 dominance violation (S-RPS only):**
- In row 20, Option B pays $15 regardless of ball drawn (dominates Option A which is a 50-50 gamble between $10 and $5)
- L-RPS: 0 of 60 subjects choose dominated Option A
- S-RPS: 4 of 61 subjects choose dominated Option A (Fisher exact p=0.119, not significant but suggestive of confusion/fatigue in separated format)

### Method
- **Design:** 20-row MPL, each row is a binary choice between a "safe" lottery l_0 (50-50 chance of $10 or $5) and a "risky" lottery l_i ($15 if ball number <= i, else $0), where i indexes the row (1 to 20). Payment determined by Bingo cage draw of 20 balls.
- **Subjects:** 181 in Experiment 1, 124 in Experiment 2, all from Ohio State University experimental economics pool. Sessions: Oct 2013 - Mar 2014.
- **Treatments (5 total across 2 experiments):**
  - L-RPS: All 20 rows on one screen as a list; one random row paid via 20-sided die
  - L-14: All 20 rows on one screen; only row 14 paid (Framed Control)
  - O-14: Only row 14 shown; only row 14 paid (One Choice baseline)
  - S-RPS: Each row on a separate screen, random order; one random row paid
  - S-14: Each row on a separate screen, random order; only row 14 paid
- **Monotonicity test logic:** Under monotonicity, RPS and Framed Control (pay only row 14) should produce identical choices in row 14, because monotonicity ensures the other rows' presence doesn't affect the optimal row-14 choice. If row-14 choices differ, monotonicity is violated and RPS is not IC.
- **Key innovation:** The Framed Control treatment (L-14 or S-14) isolates the monotonicity test from the framing effect test. Prior literature compared RPS to One Choice, which confounds the two.
- **Power:** Targeted 70% power, chose N=60 per treatment based on power calculation assuming effect sizes from Starmer & Sugden (1991). Used chi-squared test partitioned following Siegel & Castellan (1988).

### Relevance to Our Project

1. **Monotonicity is the key IC assumption, and it can be violated by presentation format.** This is directly relevant to our BDM belief elicitation project because the BDM mechanism also relies on monotonicity (Azrieli et al. 2018). If monotonicity can be violated simply by showing all BDM prices on one screen vs. separately, then the format of our BDM elicitation (slider? list of prices? single question with random price?) matters for IC.

2. **"Separated decisions" = each choice on its own screen, in random order.** This is the precise fix that restores IC. For our BDM design, this implies that presenting subjects with one belief-elicitation question at a time (rather than a list of questions) may be important for IC. Our Qualtrics survey already does this naturally (one question per screen), which is good.

3. **The confound between monotonicity violation and framing effects can hide IC failure.** In their data, comparing L-RPS to O-14 gives p=0.485 -- no evidence of IC failure. But this is because the monotonicity violation (which shifts choices toward safe) and the list framing effect (which shifts choices toward risky) nearly cancel out. Without the Framed Control treatment, this IC failure would be invisible. Lesson: we need the right comparison to detect IC failure in BDM.

4. **Multiple switching (switch-backs) increases with separated presentation but is NOT evidence against IC.** Under separated presentation, IC holds, so the increased switch-backs reflect genuine preference intransitivities (noisy indifference), not mechanism failure. This has implications for interpreting "inconsistent" BDM responses -- if subjects give different belief reports across repetitions, that may reflect measurement error in preferences rather than IC failure.

5. **The RPS/BDM connection is structural.** RPS randomly selects one of many decisions for payment; BDM randomly selects one price and compares it to the stated value. Both rely on monotonicity for IC. Brown & Healy's result that list presentation violates monotonicity in RPS suggests list-based BDM (e.g., an MPL-style BDM where subjects see all possible prices) may also violate monotonicity, while a single-response BDM (state your value, one random price drawn) may preserve it.

6. **Table 9 provides a clean taxonomy of the prior literature.** Every test using list format rejects IC; every test using separated format fails to reject IC. This is the most important empirical pattern for motivating our project's attention to presentation format.

### CS Comments


### Open Questions
- Does the list-vs-separated distinction apply to BDM for beliefs? BDM for beliefs typically uses a single response (state your probability), not a list of 20 decisions. But some implementations use an MPL-like format (e.g., Hao & Houser 2012's price list version). Would those MPL-BDM implementations fail IC like L-RPS does?
- The separated format restores IC but increases switch-backs from 5% to 33%. Is this a problem for measurement? If we use BDM in separated format and subjects give noisy responses, we get IC but lose precision. Is there an optimal number of repetitions to average over?
- Brown & Healy note that "the two effects almost perfectly cancel out" in their data (monotonicity violation and list framing). Is this coincidental, or is there a structural reason these two effects would be of similar magnitude in opposite directions? If structural, it would explain why prior L-RPS vs O-14 comparisons failed to detect IC violations.
- The monotonicity violation in L-RPS goes in the direction of choosing the safe option MORE often (51.7% risky in L-RPS vs 70.0% in L-14). This means the list format makes subjects more risk-averse when facing RPS. Why? Is it because seeing the compound lottery structure of RPS on one screen triggers a reduction-of-compound-lotteries heuristic that violates monotonicity?
- Four subjects in S-RPS chose the dominated option in row 20 (where Option B pays $15 regardless). This suggests some confusion or fatigue in the separated format. Would comprehension checks mitigate this? Our BDM design should include understanding checks.
- Mechanism invariance is assumed throughout (the payment mechanism itself doesn't alter preferences). Brown & Healy acknowledge this cannot be tested. If mechanism invariance fails, their monotonicity test is confounded. Is there independent evidence on mechanism invariance for RPS/BDM-like mechanisms?

## 21. Tsakas (2019) — Obvious Belief Elicitation

**Citation:** Tsakas, E. (2019). Obvious belief elicitation. *Games and Economic Behavior*, 118, 374-381.
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/tsakas_2019_obvious_belief_elicitation.pdf`

### Summary
Applies Li's (2017) concept of obvious strategy-proofness (OSP) to the Karni (2009) belief elicitation mechanism. Shows that Karni's ascending mechanism (strategically equivalent to a Dutch/descending clock auction) is dominance eliciting but NOT obviously dominance eliciting. Introduces a novel descending Karni mechanism (strategically equivalent to an English/ascending clock auction) that IS fully obvious dominance eliciting. Results hold for a very broad class of likelihood relations, well beyond probabilistic beliefs.

### Key Findings
- **Theorem 1:** The ascending Karni mechanism is fully dominance eliciting, but NOT obvious dominance eliciting. Truthful reporting is a dominant strategy, but it is not *obviously* dominant. The intuition: at some information sets in the ascending mechanism, the best-case outcome from deviating (exiting later) is better than the worst-case outcome from following the dominant strategy (exiting now), so OSP fails.
- **Theorem 2:** The descending Karni mechanism is fully obvious dominance eliciting. Every dominant strategy is also obviously dominant. The intuition: the descending mechanism is a personal clock auction (Li 2017, Thm. 3), and all obviously strategy-proof mechanisms take this form.
- **Static mechanisms rarely have OSP:** Section 4.3 shows that the static (normal-form) versions of both Karni mechanisms rarely have obviously dominant strategies. This is because OSP requires the condition to hold at the first information set, which in a static mechanism means it must hold globally -- a much stronger requirement. This explains why dynamic (clock) implementations are necessary for OSP.
- **Auction analogy (Section 4.5):** The ascending Karni mechanism is *strategically* equivalent to a Dutch (descending-price) auction, and the descending Karni mechanism to an English (ascending-price) auction. Note: this refers to strategic equivalence (same normal form), NOT format equivalence — the ascending Karni mechanism *looks like* an ascending clock but is strategically equivalent to a Dutch auction. Both the descending Karni mechanism and the English auction have obviously dominant strategies, while the ascending Karni mechanism and the Dutch auction do not.
- **Preference generality:** Results do not require expected utility or even probabilistic beliefs. The subject needs only a likelihood relation (qualitative ranking of events) satisfying weak comparative probability (Savage 1954). The mechanism works for any complete, transitive preferences satisfying a very mild monotonicity-with-respect-to-set-inclusion axiom.

### Method
Theory paper. Formal proofs using Li (2017) obvious dominance framework applied to discrete-time Karni mechanisms. No experiments.

### Relevance to Our Project
- **Directly explains why Hao & Houser's clock is NOT OSP.** Hao & Houser (2012) use an ascending clock mechanism, which Tsakas proves is NOT obviously dominance eliciting (Theorem 1). Chakraborty & Kendall (2025) cite this result. This is important because Hao & Houser's clock was designed to make the mechanism simpler, but Tsakas shows it fails the formal simplicity criterion. The fact that it still improves behavior (Hao & Houser find it helps) suggests that even non-OSP dynamic mechanisms can be behaviorally helpful -- or that OSP is too demanding a criterion for belief elicitation.
- **The descending mechanism is the OSP variant.** If we wanted to test whether OSP matters for belief elicitation, we would need to implement the *descending* Karni mechanism (start at certainty, decrease), not the ascending one. This has not been done experimentally.
- **Connects to the BDM-is-a-Dutch-auction analogy.** The ascending Karni mechanism (which is what most experimenters implement as "BDM for beliefs") is equivalent to a Dutch auction. Cason & Plott (2014) showed that subjects confuse BDM-for-values with first-price auctions. Tsakas's framework suggests this confusion may be related to the non-obvious nature of the dominant strategy in Dutch-like mechanisms.
- **Static BDM is even worse.** Section 4.3 confirms that the static (direct revelation) version of the mechanism -- which is what most belief elicitation experiments actually use -- almost never has obviously dominant strategies. This provides theoretical backing for the conjecture that subjects struggle with static BDM precisely because the dominant strategy is not obvious.
- **Framework for our theory section.** Tsakas provides the formal definitions (Definitions 3-6) that let us precisely state what kind of incentive compatibility the BDM mechanism has (dominance but not obvious dominance) and what kind the descending variant would have (obvious dominance). This taxonomy is useful for positioning our project.

### Open Questions
- The descending Karni mechanism has never been experimentally tested for belief elicitation. Would it actually perform better than the ascending version? Tsakas's theory predicts yes (OSP), but the Burfurd & Wilkening (2018) result that making BDM-for-values obviously strategy-proof (via MPL) does not fully eliminate misconception suggests the answer may be more nuanced.
- Tsakas works with discrete approximations to the continuous Karni mechanism. How fine does the grid need to be for the approximation to be adequate? In practice, experimenters use 100-point scales (percentages) -- is this fine enough?
- The paper focuses on single-event probability elicitation. Section 4.4 discusses extending to multiple events but notes that the obvious dominance property does not extend directly. For full probability distributions, the mechanism would need to be run separately for each event, which is cumbersome.
- Why does the ascending Karni mechanism fail OSP but still have a dominant strategy? The intuition is about regret: at some decision points, the subject can imagine a scenario where deviating would have been better (even though in expectation it's worse). This connects to the behavioral question of whether subjects reason about worst-case vs. expected outcomes.

## 22. Holt & Smith (2009) — An Update on Bayesian Updating

**Citation:** Holt, C.A. & Smith, A.M. (2009). An update on Bayesian updating. *Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization*, 69(2), 125--134.
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Holt_Smith_2009_JEBO.pdf`

### Summary

Holt & Smith conduct both hand-run (N=22) and web-based (N=96) experiments in which subjects observe draws with replacement from one of two colored-marble "cups" and report their subjective probability that a specific cup was selected. The key methodological contribution is a Becker-DeGroot-Marshak elicitation procedure using a "dice lottery" framing: subjects report "chances out of 100" that cup A is being used, two 10-sided dice generate a random number N, and the comparison of N to the report determines whether the subject receives a "cup A lottery" or an "N lottery." This BDM procedure avoids risk-neutrality assumptions that plague quadratic scoring rules. The paper uses this design to document systematic deviations from Bayesian updating -- particularly base-rate neglect, representativeness bias, and probability weighting -- across treatments varying the prior probability of cup A (1/3, 1/2, 2/3, and 4/100).

### Key Findings

- **Aggregate behavior approximates Bayesian predictions for symmetric priors (1/2),** with data falling close to the 45-degree line (Fig. 1). For asymmetric priors (1/3, 2/3), deviations are more systematic.
- **Base-rate neglect:** In asymmetric prior treatments, subjects underweight prior information. When the prior is 2/3, predictions are biased downward (subjects behave as if the prior were closer to 1/2). Fig. 5 shows data falls between the correct Bayesian prediction and the incorrect prediction based on a 1/2 prior -- confirming partial but incomplete use of prior information.
- **Representativeness bias:** When a 3-draw sequence matches the composition of cup A (e.g., 2 reds + 1 blue from a cup containing 2 red + 1 blue), subjects report higher probabilities than the Bayesian posterior warrants. When the sequence matches cup B's composition, they report lower probabilities (Tables 5--6). Replicates Grether (1980, 1992).
- **Probability weighting (OLS estimation, Table 7):** Log-linear model estimates show coefficient on log prior odds = 0.713 (SE 0.024), significantly below 1, indicating overweighting of low prior probabilities and underweighting of high priors. Coefficient on log likelihood ratio = 1.027 (SE 0.024), not significantly different from 1 -- subjects fully incorporate sample evidence but underweight priors. R-squared = 0.504 (N=2,395).
- **Large individual heterogeneity (Fig. 7):** Individual-level estimation of a single probability weighting parameter gamma across 96 web-based subjects yields a wide range from near 0 to above 2.5. Only 45% (43/96) have gamma not significantly different from 1 (Bayesian). Five subjects have gamma > 2.0, implying systematic overweighting of all posterior probabilities.
- **Extreme priors amplify deviations:** With prior = 4/100, overweighting of low probabilities is much more severe (Fig. 6), and the probability weighting model with intermediate priors fails to capture these large upward deviations.
- **Reinforcing draws produce monotonic updating:** Subjects update beliefs in the correct direction when observing reinforcing draws of the same color (Tables 3--4), though the magnitude may not match Bayesian predictions exactly.

### Method

- **Experiment I (hand-run):** N=22, UVA economics classes. Cup A: 2 light + 1 dark marble; Cup B: 1 light + 2 dark. Draws with replacement. 3 sequences varying priors (1/2, 2/3, 1/2; order reversed for half). 27 decisions (short) or 30 (extended). Physical dice, cups, and marbles. Several practice rounds with dice throws, draws, and earnings calculation.
- **Experiment II (web-based):** N=72 (24 per prior: 1/3, 1/2, 2/3), between-subjects, using Veconlab Bayes' Rule program. 30 decisions per subject across 3 sequences. Red/blue balls instead of light/dark marbles. Simulated dice and draws presented simultaneously. A fourth treatment (N=24) with prior = 4/100 used a 100-sided simulated die (4 sides cup A, 96 sides cup B; cup A = all red, cup B = 3 red + 7 blue).
- **BDM elicitation ("dice lottery" framing):** Subject reports R = "chances out of 100" that cup A is being used. Two 10-sided dice generate N (0--99). If N >= R, the "N lottery" pays $1 with probability N/100 (determined by a second random draw from 100 marked ping pong balls). If N < R, the "cup A lottery" pays $1 if cup A was actually selected. IC logic: truthful reporting maximizes the probability of receiving the preferred lottery. Stakes: $6 show-up + $1 per selected round.
- **Footnote 5 (comprehension baseline):** In initial no-draw rounds of the web experiment (prior is the only information), exactly 75% of reports fell within the correct interval -- meaning **25% of subjects could not correctly report the stated prior through the BDM mechanism** even with no updating required.

### The BDM "Dice Lottery" Framing in Detail

The exact elicitation procedure -- which becomes the "HS" format in Burfurd & Wilkening (2018) -- works as follows:

1. Subject reports R = "chances out of 100" that cup A is being used (0 = no chance, 50 = equal, 100 = certainty).
2. Experimenter throws two 10-sided dice to generate N (tens digit, then ones digit), uniform on 0--99.
3. **If N >= R:** The N lottery is used. It pays $V (the prize amount) with probability N/100, determined by a second random draw.
4. **If N < R:** The cup A lottery is used. It pays $V if cup A was actually the selected cup, $0 otherwise.
5. **IC argument presented to subjects:** "It is best for people to report their true 'chances out of 100' that cup A was being used." The intuition: the cup A lottery is "worth" exactly your true belief (pays $V with probability = true belief cup A was used). The N lottery pays $V with probability N/100. You want whichever lottery has the higher probability of paying, so you want the cup A lottery when N/100 < your belief (N < R) and the N lottery when N/100 > your belief (N >= R). Setting R = true belief achieves exactly this partition.
6. **Practice:** In Experiment I, "several practice decision rounds" with physical dice, cups, and marbles. In Experiment II, physical demonstrations before the computerized sessions. Despite this, "participants did have some questions" (p. 127) and "subjects did not seem to have trouble grasping the idea, especially after several decision rounds."

**Key design choice:** The procedure was "not couched in probability terms" -- subjects reported "chances out of 100" rather than probabilities, following the frequency framing advocated by Gigerenzer and Hoffrage (1995).

### Relevance to Our Project

1. **This is the origin of the "HS" (Holt-Smith) instruction format.** Burfurd & Wilkening (2018) explicitly adopt this instruction format as one of three treatments (HS, Grether, BW). The HS format is defined by: (a) "chances out of 100" phrasing, (b) two 10-sided dice for the random cutoff, (c) comparison between a "cup A lottery" and an "N lottery." B&W (2018) find that the HS format produces the weakest comprehension quiz performance among the three formats, making this paper the baseline against which instruction improvements are measured.

2. **The 25% comprehension failure rate (footnote 5) is critical evidence for our project.** Even in no-draw rounds -- where no Bayesian updating is required and subjects just need to report the stated prior through the mechanism -- 25% fail. This directly supports our thesis that BDM comprehension is a binding constraint on behavioral incentive compatibility. If a quarter of subjects cannot correctly use the mechanism even for the simplest possible task, theoretical IC (Karni 2009) is behaviorally irrelevant for those subjects.

3. **The probability weighting results may partly reflect mechanism confusion.** The key finding that gamma_1 (prior weighting) = 0.713 while gamma_2 (likelihood weighting) = 1.027 admits two interpretations: (a) genuine probability weighting that differs for priors vs. sample evidence, or (b) differential comprehension -- subjects intuitively understand how draws relate to cup identity but struggle to incorporate the prior into their BDM report. Our project could disentangle these by comparing BDM reports to a simpler mechanism (e.g., flat fee) on the same updating task. If "probability weighting" disappears under flat fee, it was mechanism confusion, not genuine weighting.

4. **The large individual heterogeneity (Fig. 7) maps to Burfurd & Wilkening (2022).** H&S find 45% of subjects behave as Bayesians while others show extreme deviations. B&W (2022) formalize this as a mixture of "sophisticated" and "naive" types. The connection: are the non-Bayesian subjects in H&S failing at Bayesian updating, or failing to understand the BDM? Our design (same updating task, different elicitation mechanisms) can distinguish these explanations.

5. **The dice-lottery framing sits in a lineage of progressively more transparent BDM implementations.** Evolution: Grether (1992) "book bag and poker chip" -> H&S (2009) dice lottery -> B&W (2018) analogy format -> H&S (2016) synchronized lottery choice menu -> Brown et al. (2026) CBC payoff tables. Our project asks whether ANY instruction format makes the declarative BDM behaviorally IC, or whether the comprehension problem is inherent to the mechanism's structure.

6. **Extensive practice did not fully solve comprehension.** Despite physical demonstrations with actual cups, marbles, and dice (much richer than typical online experiments), 25% still failed the simplest test. This suggests the comprehension problem is not merely about instruction quality -- it may be intrinsic to the mechanism's cognitive demands (understanding that a probability comparison determines lottery selection, and that truthful reporting optimizes this comparison).

### CS Comments


### Open Questions
- The 25% failure rate in no-draw rounds (footnote 5) uses generous coding: reports are "correct" if within [30, 35] for prior = 1/3 and [65, 70] for prior = 2/3. With tighter criteria, the failure rate would be higher. What fraction of subjects report exactly the prior (33 or 67)?
- H&S interpret all deviations as "probability weighting" or "representativeness bias." But mechanism confusion could mimic probability weighting: a subject who understands that higher numbers mean "more likely cup A" but doesn't grasp the precise IC logic may produce reports that systematically overweight low probabilities and underweight high ones (regression to the mean of the response scale). Can we formally show that a "noisy comprehension" model generates the same estimation signature as probability weighting in the H&S log-linear framework?
- The estimation model (Eq. 5) constrains probability weighting to have the same functional form for overweighting and underweighting. The asymmetry between prior weighting (0.71) and likelihood weighting (1.03) is across information sources, but within-source asymmetry is also possible.
- Stakes are $1 per round with 27--30 rounds and $6 show-up. Per-round incentive is small. Does this provide sufficient motivation for subjects to invest in understanding the BDM mechanism? Gneezy & Rustichini (2000) suggest small incentives can be worse than none.
- The web experiment used simulated dice rather than physical dice. Could mistrust of computerized randomization explain some deviations? This concern is less relevant for our project (which also uses online elicitation) but relevant for interpreting H&S's comparison between hand-run and web results.
- H&S note that the BDM is "intuitive but complicated to implement" (p. 127). They resolve this tension by providing extensive practice. But practice rounds may teach subjects to report correctly through feedback-based learning rather than genuine comprehension of the IC logic. If so, early-round data (before learning) may better reflect the mechanism's intrinsic comprehensibility, while late-round data reflects learned behavior. Does the paper report learning effects across rounds?

## 23. Grether (1981) — Financial Incentive Effects and Individual Decisionmaking

**Citation:** Grether, D.M. (1981). Financial incentive effects and individual decisionmaking. *Social Science Working Paper No. 401*, California Institute of Technology.
**Read date:** April 6, 2026
**PDF:** `master_supporting_docs/literature/papers/Grether_1981_sswp401.pdf`

### Summary
One of the earliest uses of a BDM-like mechanism for eliciting subjective probabilities in economics. Grether uses a variant of the Becker-DeGroot-Marschak (1964) procedure to incentivize probability reports in a representativeness heuristic experiment. The paper's primary contribution is demonstrating that financial incentives produce qualitatively different behavior from unincentivized reports -- not merely reduced variance, but fundamentally different patterns of response. Subjects without incentives made significantly more errors, showed more confusion, and were more likely to be nonresponsive.

### Key Findings
- **BDM-for-beliefs mechanism:** Subjects observed draws from one of two bingo cages (Cage A or Cage B) with different compositions, then reported their subjective probability that the draws came from Cage A. They circled a number from 0 to 50 on a form. This number determined which bet they played: if the number circled was less than a randomly drawn number from Cage Y (containing balls 1-50), the subject played the "Number Bet" (objective lottery); otherwise they played the "Cage Bet" (pays if they identified the correct cage). Grether explicitly notes this is "a variant of the method of Gordon Becker, DeGroot, and Marschak (1964) for determining demand prices" and states "it is a dominant strategy for the subjects to reveal their true personal probabilities."
- **Between-subjects incentive manipulation:** 99 UCLA economics students. One room (N=48) received financial incentives: one decision randomly selected, correct bet won $15 vs. $5. Other room (N=49) received flat $7 payment regardless of decisions. Procedures identical otherwise.
- **Incentives reduce errors dramatically:** With incentives, 42/960 responses (4.4%) were clearly in error; without incentives, 126/980 responses (12.9%) were clearly in error. Chi-squared = 44.1, highly significant.
- **Incentives reduce confusion:** 15/48 incentivized subjects made some errors vs. 32/49 unincentivized subjects (Table 2B). Note: Table 2B accounts for only 97 of the 99 participants — 2 subjects are unaccounted for, likely excluded but not discussed. This is not just a few people making many errors -- the error rate is distributed across many more individuals without incentives.
- **Qualitative, not quantitative, differences:** The key finding is that behavior without incentives is not merely noisier -- it is qualitatively different. Some unincentivized subjects circled 50 for every trial (nonresponsive), indicated probabilities of unity (impossible given the design), or showed no sensitivity to the data. These patterns were much rarer with incentives. Grether emphasizes this is not a "fine tuning" effect that larger samples could fix.
- **After dropping questionable responses:** Using a criterion of 5+ clear errors out of ~20 responses, 167/960 (17.4%) incentivized responses were dropped vs. 398/980 (40.6%) unincentivized responses. Chi-squared = 126.6.

### Method
Lab experiment. N=99 UCLA economics undergraduates. Between-subjects: financial incentives ($15/$5 for correct/incorrect bet, one randomly selected) vs. flat payment ($7). Task: observe 6 draws from a bingo cage with known prior odds (1/2, 1, or 2 favoring Cage A), report posterior probability that draws came from Cage A by circling a number 0-50. Each subject completed ~20 trials with varying priors and data. BDM-variant mechanism for incentive compatibility. Dependent variables: error rates, confusion rates, nonresponsiveness.

### Relevance to Our Project
- **Historical priority as first BDM-for-beliefs in economics.** Grether (1981) is, to our knowledge, the first paper in economics to use a BDM-like mechanism specifically for eliciting subjective probabilities. He explicitly connects his procedure to BDM (1964) and states the dominant strategy property. This predates Karni (2009) by 28 years, though Karni provided the formal proof of IC under weaker assumptions. Ducharme & Donnell (1973) used a similar "bets mode" in psychology even earlier, but Grether is the economics precedent.
- **Early evidence on BDM comprehension problems.** Even with Grether's relatively simple implementation (circle a number 0-50), subjects made systematic errors. With incentives, 4.4% of responses were clearly wrong; without incentives, 12.9%. The "clearly in error" responses are ones where a subject's circled number was inconsistent with the BDM dominant strategy given any reasonable belief. This suggests that even in 1981, experimenters recognized that BDM required subjects to understand a non-trivial mapping from beliefs to actions.
- **The incentive effect is qualitative, not just quantitative.** Grether's finding that unincentivized behavior is *qualitatively different* (not just noisier) foreshadows the BIC literature's core insight. If incentives merely reduced variance, mechanism design would be less important. But if incentives change the *type* of behavior, then the mechanism matters.
- **The mechanism is essentially static BDM.** Subjects circled a number on a form -- this is the static (normal-form) version of the Karni mechanism. Tsakas (2019) proves this version almost never has obviously dominant strategies. Grether's error rates are consistent with this theoretical prediction.
- **Caltech experimental economics tradition.** This paper comes from the Grether-Plott Caltech group that pioneered experimental economics. The companion paper Grether (1980, QJE) analyzes the representativeness heuristic results in detail; this working paper focuses on the incentive manipulation. The experimental methodology (elected monitor, practice rounds, random payment) reflects early best practices.

### CS Comments


### Open Questions
- Grether states the dominant strategy property but does not prove it formally or cite a proof. He simply asserts it is "a variant of the method of Gordon Becker, DeGroot, and Marschak (1964)" -- implying the IC argument carries over from values to beliefs. Was this recognized as requiring additional assumptions (e.g., probabilistic sophistication) at the time, or was it taken as self-evident? The formal proof had to wait for Karni (2009).
- The error rate of 4.4% even WITH incentives suggests that BDM comprehension is imperfect even under ideal conditions. Is this a floor effect of the mechanism, or could better instructions/training reduce it further?
- Grether uses a 0-50 scale, not 0-100. The mapping from the circled number to subjective probability requires subjects to understand that circling k means their probability is between k/50 and (k+1)/50 for one bet, or involves comparing to a random draw from 1-50. Is this added layer of abstraction (compared to directly reporting a percentage) contributing to confusion?
- The paper is a Caltech SSWP (working paper) and was apparently never published in a journal. Why? Was it absorbed into Grether (1980, QJE) or Grether & Plott (1979, AER), or was it a standalone that didn't find a journal home? This matters for citation purposes.
- How does the 4.4% error rate under BDM compare to error rates under proper scoring rules in similar tasks from the same era? If scoring rules produced similar error rates, the problem is task difficulty; if BDM produces more errors, the problem is mechanism complexity.
## Cross-Paper Themes & Connections

*Updated after reading 23 papers. Each theme cites specific papers and findings.*

### Theme 1: Comprehension is the binding constraint, not preferences
Across every paper that tests BDM behaviorally, comprehension failure emerges as the dominant explanation for BIC failure:
- Hao & Houser (2012): 53% non-optimal in declarative/BDM even with known beliefs, physical props, and quizzes
- Burfurd & Wilkening (2018): Dropping the quiz nearly doubles error (11.2 → 20.4) and causes 26% boundary reports
- Burdea & Woon (2022): BDM has 47% first-attempt quiz accuracy vs. 73% BSR; BDM accuracy is numerically worst
- Holt & Smith (2009): 25% of subjects cannot correctly report the stated prior through BDM, even in no-draw rounds where the answer is given
- Martin & Munoz-Rodriguez (2022): Contingent-thinking reframing (the original CBC) improves BDM bidding by reducing perception costs. Caveat: improvement depends on initial misperception — subjects with extreme FPA priors could get further from the correct bid
- Chakraborty & Kendall (2025): BDM is not UJS — many non-dominant actions are "justifiable" because subjects can rationalize them by considering specific contingencies
- Tsakas (2019): Static BDM almost never has obviously dominant strategies; even the ascending clock (Karni mechanism) is dominant but NOT obviously dominant
- Brown et al. (2026): CBC simplification of BDM only works with ≤7 states; PL format outperforms regardless of simplicity refinement
- Karni (2009): The IC proof *assumes* subjects can evaluate the event bet as equivalent to a lottery with their subjective probability — this implicit comprehension assumption is never stated formally but is the cognitive bottleneck

**Important caveat:** Not all BIC failure is comprehension. Benoit, Dubra & Romagnoli (2022) show that nonmonetary motives — preference for control and desire for positive self-statements — account for 68% of apparent "overconfidence" in matching probabilities. But this operates only in self-belief settings; in induced-probability designs (urn draws), the control channel should be absent, isolating comprehension as the remaining explanation.

The consistent picture for induced-probability designs: BDM's theoretical IC advantage (weakest assumptions) is offset by the fact that subjects cannot identify the dominant strategy. The mechanism that requires the least from preferences demands the most from cognition. The theory (Karni 2009, Azrieli et al. 2018) is entirely normative — it says nothing about whether subjects can navigate the mechanism. Healy (2020) argues the solution is to explain BDM as a list of binary choices (his "RBC" framing), making the implicit contingent reasoning explicit.

### Theme 2: Format matters more than formal IC properties
Multiple papers find that HOW the mechanism is presented matters more than its theoretical IC properties:
- Holt & Smith (2016): The LC (synchronized lottery choice menu = discretized BDM as MPL) outperforms single-response BDM on exact Bayesian responses (35.7% vs. 20.0%, p=0.021) and boundary decisions (3% vs. 10%, p=0.046), despite being theoretically identical
- Brown et al. (2026): Price list format outperforms BDM and clock regardless of simplicity refinements (CBC, OSP, GSO/UJS)
- Hao & Houser (2012): Clock (sequential binary) outperforms declarative (single number) despite identical IC conditions — both through censoring of naive responses and better strategy adoption among non-censored subjects
- Brown & Healy (2018): Monotonicity is violated when decisions are shown as a list on one screen (p=0.041), but NOT when each is shown on a separate screen (p=0.697). Presentation format determines whether the IC assumption itself holds.
- Burfurd & Wilkening (2018): Format differences (HS vs. HH vs. TK) don't affect accuracy once quizzes are included — but quizzes themselves are essential
- Burdea & Woon (2022): BSR outperforms BDM on distributional properties (fewer 50% beliefs, beliefs moved in the right direction) despite stronger IC assumptions. Note: average accuracy is NOT significantly different — the advantage is in belief *quality* (distribution shape), not *accuracy* (match to ground truth).
- Burdea & Woon (2022): Incentivized beliefs are 12-16pp *less* accurate than unincentivized numeracy items with identical probabilities — the mechanism's cognitive cost may offset its theoretical benefit.
- Gonzalez-Fernandez et al. (2025): Unincentivized slider method produces better-calibrated beliefs than incentivized bins

The implication: behavioral IC is a property of the mechanism-as-implemented, not the mechanism-as-theorized. Our project should frame BIC failure as a joint property of the incentive structure AND the presentation format. Brown & Healy's (2018) finding is particularly striking — format doesn't just affect accuracy, it determines whether the IC *assumption itself* (monotonicity) is satisfied.

### Theme 3: Cognitive heterogeneity concentrates BIC failures
- Burfurd & Wilkening (2022): SBDM amplifies heterogeneity in probabilistic reasoning (37.6% smaller error for consistent vs. inconsistent) but NOT in general intelligence or effort
- Burdea & Woon (2022): Numeracy is the single strongest predictor of accuracy (−42.9pp for low numeracy), dwarfing treatment effects
- Holt & Smith (2009): Only 45% of subjects (43/96) behave as Bayesians; the rest show systematic biases in prior weighting
- Dustan et al. (2023): Only 30% of participants consistently "reduce" compound lotteries — but even fixing reduction (ROE) doesn't improve accuracy
- Grether (1981): Even the earliest BDM-for-beliefs experiment found qualitative differences between incentivized and unincentivized subjects — 4.4% error rate with incentives vs. 12.9% without, and unincentivized behavior is qualitatively different (nonresponsive, impossible reports), not just noisier
- Hao & Houser (2012): Experience partially closes the gap, suggesting the "naive" population can learn

The binding cognitive skill is specifically *probabilistic/mechanism reasoning*, not general intelligence. BIC failures are concentrated among subjects who struggle with this specific skill, and these subjects are a majority in online populations.

### Theme 4: Experience helps but doesn't fully solve the problem
Multiple papers show that comprehension improves with experience, but residual strategic confusion persists:
- Hao & Houser (2012): The accuracy gap between declarative and clock closes after one round. Among all observations: declarative improves from 47% → 57% optimal; clock improves from 39% → 60% optimal (round 2 difference n.s., p=0.84). But distributional differences persist (p=0.052) — experienced subjects still use different strategies under the two mechanisms.
- Burfurd & Wilkening (2018): No-quiz subjects initially make boundary reports (26%) then learn over time, but they start from a much worse position. Even with quizzes, mean absolute error remains 11-13pp (far from perfect).
- Brown et al. (2026): PL facilitates learning better than BDM — by the second round, PL-based mechanisms greatly outperform all others on both accuracy metrics. BDM learning is slower.
- Burfurd & Wilkening (2022): Even with HH format + quizzes + 40 periods, inconsistent probabilistic reasoners continue to show larger errors under SBDM. The gap does not close with experience for this subgroup.

The implication: the BDM's incentive logic is genuinely harder to internalize than alternatives. One-shot scaffolding (quizzes, instructions) closes the initial comprehension gap, but some subjects never fully learn the mechanism. This is relevant to our design: if we use repeated rounds, we should expect learning effects, but we should not expect BIC failure to disappear entirely even with extensive practice.

### Theme 5: The theory-behavior gap is the core puzzle
The theoretical literature (Karni 2009, Azrieli et al. 2018, Tsakas 2019, Chakraborty & Kendall 2025) provides increasingly precise formal analyses of mechanism properties. The empirical literature (Hao & Houser 2012, Burfurd & Wilkening 2018/2022, Burdea & Woon 2022, Holt & Smith 2009/2016, Brown & Healy 2018, Grether 1981) consistently documents behavioral failures. The gap between these two literatures is the core puzzle:
- Karni (2009) proves IC under probabilistic sophistication + dominance. Azrieli et al. (2018) prove RPS IC under the even weaker assumption of monotonicity. Yet 47-53% of subjects fail to play optimally.
- Tsakas (2019) shows static BDM almost never has obviously dominant strategies, providing formal grounding for why comprehension fails. Chakraborty & Kendall (2025) show BDM is not UJS — the dominant strategy is not the only justifiable action.
- Brown & Healy (2018) show that even the IC *assumption* (monotonicity) depends on presentation format. The theory assumes monotonicity holds; the data show it holds only under specific presentation conditions.
- The theoretical lineage (Savage 1971 [scoring rules, no reading notes] → Ducharme & Donnell 1973 [first SBDM experiment, no reading notes] → Grether 1981 → Karni 2009 → Azrieli et al. 2018) has progressively weakened the assumptions needed for IC. But the behavioral evidence has not improved — if anything, more diverse subject pools (online, MTurk) show *worse* behavioral IC than student labs.

Our project sits squarely in this gap: we take the mechanism that theory says should work best (weakest IC assumptions) and ask WHY it fails behaviorally. The answer, synthesized across 23 papers, points to comprehension/format as the primary channel, with cognitive heterogeneity as the moderator and experience as an incomplete remedy.

## Implications for Research Direction

*Updated after reading 23 papers.*

1. **The comprehension hypothesis has strong empirical support across multiple labs, platforms, and decades** (Grether 1981 through Burdea & Woon 2022). It is no longer speculative — BDM comprehension failure is well-documented. Our contribution should be identifying the *specific* cognitive bottleneck (contingent reasoning? probabilistic mechanism reasoning? game-form misconception?) and testing whether it can be fixed.

2. **BDM vs. flat fee may be the most important comparison.** Burdea & Woon find BDM does not outperform flat-fee elicitation. If this holds in our setting, the practical case for BDM collapses entirely.

3. **BDM vs. MPL is the cleanest format comparison.** Holt & Smith (2016) show the LC (= MPL format of BDM) outperforms single-response BDM despite theoretical equivalence. This isolates the *format* effect from the *incentive* effect. Our design should include this comparison.

4. **Quiz/training effects are first-order.** Burfurd & Wilkening's quiz result (26% boundary reports without quiz) suggests that comprehension scaffolding is more important than the choice of mechanism. A treatment arm varying comprehension support (quiz, worked examples, practice rounds) would be high-value.

5. **The clock mechanism provides a useful benchmark** for understanding BDM failures (Hao & Houser), but for our diagnostic purposes we need to *observe* errors, not censor them. BDM's weakness is our strength for studying BIC failure.

6. **Monotonicity compliance depends on format** (Brown & Healy 2018). Our design should use separated decisions (one per screen) to ensure monotonicity holds, so that any BIC failure we observe is due to *comprehension* of the specific mechanism, not a general failure of the IC assumption. This is a methodological requirement, not just a design choice.

7. **The theory-behavior gap is our paper's framing.** The theoretical IC hierarchy is well-established (Karni 2009, Azrieli et al. 2018, Healy & Leo 2025). The behavioral failures are well-documented. What's missing is a systematic diagnosis of WHY the gap exists for the mechanism with the weakest theoretical demands. This is our contribution.
